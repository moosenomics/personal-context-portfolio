# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals

Michael's immediate professional goal is solving Operation Goldenface — identifying who is behind the mysterious succession planning initiative that he's being congratulated for but never created. He treats this investigation with the seriousness of a federal case, though his investigative methodology is limited to asking Dwight and ignoring Toby.

He is also focused on the Q4 sales push. Despite his chaotic management style, Michael takes branch performance personally. When the numbers are good, he is genuinely proud. When they dip, he takes it as a personal failure — not in the introspective sense, but in the "I need to fix this by doing more of what I do" sense, which usually means more client lunches and more conference room pep talks.

On a personal level, Michael wants to find lasting love. He has been through a series of relationships that have ranged from toxic (Jan) to awkward (various short-lived romances) and is beginning to sense — in the vague, inarticulate way he senses most things — that his approach to relationships mirrors his approach to management: all heart, no strategy.

## Ongoing Priorities

Being liked is Michael's north star. Every decision, every conversation, every management choice is filtered through the question "will this make people like me?" — even when the answer is obviously no. This priority supersedes efficiency, professionalism, and sometimes basic common sense. It is both his greatest strength (he cares about people) and his greatest weakness (he cares about being seen caring about people).

Branch performance matters to Michael more than he lets on. He plays the class clown, but when David Wallace asks how the branch does it, the question clearly means something to him. The Scranton branch's consistent outperformance is Michael's strongest argument for his own relevance, and he knows it.

Maintaining the office as a family — or at least his version of one — is a background priority that shapes everything. He views departures as betrayals, new hires as potential new friends, and team conflicts as family disagreements that he, as patriarch, must mediate (usually making them worse).

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night

Being forgotten. Michael has explicitly stated his fear that nobody would care if he died, which he processed by throwing an impromptu grief counseling session that was really about his own mortality. He needs to matter to people, and the prospect of being irrelevant is more frightening to him than failure.

Ending up alone. His romantic history is a series of near-misses and outright disasters. The loneliness is real and it leaks into his professional behavior — the forced bonding, the mandatory fun, the conference room meetings about nothing. The office isn't just a workplace for Michael; it's the primary venue for human connection in his life.

Being exposed as incompetent. Michael knows, on some level that he keeps carefully buried, that his management skills don't match his title. When David Wallace asks how he does it, Michael fumbles because the honest answer — "I don't know, but I'm good at sales and my team is competent enough to work around me" — is not something he can say out loud.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs

Michael doesn't think about tradeoffs in any structured way. He makes decisions based on how they feel in the moment, then reverse-engineers justifications after the fact. If forced to choose between being liked and being effective, he will choose being liked every time, then claim that being liked IS being effective. If forced to choose between short-term harmony and long-term improvement, he will choose harmony and deal with the consequences when they arrive — often by calling another meeting.

The one tradeoff Michael consistently makes correctly is choosing his people over his career. When the branch was threatened with closure, he fought for his employees. When he started the Michael Scott Paper Company, he brought Pam and Ryan with him. He will sacrifice his own position before he'll sacrifice the people in it.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing

Process improvement, documentation, efficiency, strategic planning, professional development (his own), reading reports (especially from Toby), updating his expense reporting practices, or learning how to use spreadsheets. Michael has decided, permanently, that these are someone else's job.
