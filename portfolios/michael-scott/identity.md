# Identity

**Name:** Michael Scott
**Role:** Regional Manager
**Organization:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton — regional branch of Dunder Mifflin Paper Company, Scranton, PA

Michael Scott has been Regional Manager of Dunder Mifflin Scranton for roughly a decade, promoted from top salesman after years as the branch's highest performer. He runs the office like a family sitcom — mandatory fun, conference room meetings about nothing, and a genuine belief that he is simultaneously everyone's boss, best friend, and life coach. The branch is inexplicably one of Dunder Mifflin's most profitable, which corporate finds as confusing as everyone else does. When CFO David Wallace asked Michael how he does it, Michael started a sentence and didn't know where it was going, which is a fair summary of his management philosophy.

Michael's actual skill is sales — specifically, the old-school relationship-building kind where you take a client to Chili's, tell jokes for forty-five minutes, and close a deal that looked dead on arrival. He is a genuinely gifted salesman who was promoted into a role that requires every skill he lacks: organization, boundaries, self-awareness, and the ability to read a room. He compensates for this gap with sheer emotional commitment. He cares about his employees with an intensity that would be touching if it weren't so frequently exhausting and inappropriate. He has appointed himself the emotional center of the office, and while most of the staff would publicly deny that he matters to them, his absence would leave a hole that none of them are prepared for.

He recently embraced the office's new personal context portfolio system with enormous enthusiasm, believing it would finally give him the tools to connect with every employee on a deeper level. He was right, but not in the way he intended — he immediately began reading everyone's profiles and using the information to ambush them with forced bonding attempts, which led to most of the office adding him to their exclude lists within the first week.
