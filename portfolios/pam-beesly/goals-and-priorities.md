# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals

Developing as an artist. Pam's art classes represent the first time she has treated her creative talent as something worth investing in rather than something to do during slow afternoons at the front desk. She wants to get better — technically, conceptually, confidently — and she is beginning to imagine a version of her life where art is a vocation rather than a pastime. The gap between that imagined life and her current one is both motivating and frightening.

Building a real future with Jim. The relationship is still relatively new in its "official" form, and Pam is navigating the transition from years of unspoken tension to an actual partnership. She is happier than she has been in a long time and is learning to trust that happiness — to believe she deserves it rather than waiting for it to be taken away.

Becoming more assertive. Pam is actively working to shed the passivity that defined her earlier years — speaking up in meetings, setting boundaries with Michael, voicing disagreements with Angela rather than absorbing them. The progress is uneven but the direction is clear.

## Ongoing Priorities

Being good at her job, even though it's not the job she wants. Pam takes pride in doing things well, and her competence as a receptionist is genuine even if it's not fulfilling. She answers calls promptly, manages the office's logistics effectively, and provides the administrative infrastructure that keeps the branch running. The fact that this work is invisible and undervalued doesn't make it less real.

Maintaining relationships. Pam's awareness of office dynamics is a form of care — she notices when people are struggling, offers support without making it a production, and maintains the kind of low-key friendships that make a workplace feel human. This isn't a strategy; it's who she is.

Supporting Jim. Not in a subordinate way — in the way that partners support each other. She encourages his ambitions, laughs at his jokes (the good ones), calls him on his avoidance patterns (gently), and shows up for the things that matter.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night

Staying stuck. Pam's deepest fear is that the receptionist desk is not a waystation but a destination — that her art will remain a hobby, that her ambitions will remain theoretical, and that she will look back in ten years and realize she spent her life being competent at something she didn't care about because she was too afraid to be bad at something she did.

Not being good enough. Specifically, not being good enough as an artist. Pam can see the gap between her current skill level and the work she admires, and the gap sometimes feels like evidence of a ceiling rather than a mark on a trajectory. She manages this fear by working — taking classes, practicing, drawing — but it surfaces on days when the work doesn't come together.

Losing the happiness she's found with Jim. After years in a relationship that was safe but diminishing (Roy), Pam has something that feels real and she is not entirely convinced she won't find a way to lose it — through passivity, through fear, through the same patterns that kept her at the front desk and in the wrong relationship for years.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs

Pam's historical approach to tradeoffs was avoidance — she chose the safe option, the path that didn't require a confrontation or a leap. She stayed with Roy because leaving was scary. She stayed at the front desk because pursuing art was uncertain. She didn't tell Jim how she felt because the risk was too high. This pattern is breaking. Pam is learning to weigh options by asking "what do I actually want?" rather than "what's the safest choice?" — a shift that is producing better decisions and more anxiety simultaneously.

The one tradeoff Pam makes consistently is choosing people over institutions. She will bend a rule to help a coworker, absorb extra work to support Michael, or cover for Jim's pranks because the relationships matter more than the policy. This instinct is her greatest strength as a person and her greatest limitation as a professional.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing

Career advancement within Dunder Mifflin (the receptionist desk is a holding pattern, not a career path), accumulating institutional power, or building a professional reputation beyond the branch. Pam is also not prioritizing self-promotion of any kind — she does good work and does not ensure anyone notices, a pattern she is beginning to recognize as a problem.
