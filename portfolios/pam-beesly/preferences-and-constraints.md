# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints

Pam is the sole receptionist at the Scranton branch, which means the front desk is unstaffed when she's away from it — limiting her flexibility for meetings, lunch timing, and any activity that requires leaving the building during business hours. She reports to Michael Scott, whose management style creates unpredictable demands on her time and emotional energy. Her compensation is receptionist-level despite responsibilities that substantially exceed the role, a constraint she is aware of but has not yet addressed directly.

Art classes happen in the evenings, which means her schedule is tighter than it used to be and her energy is spread across more commitments.

## Strong Preferences

Pam prefers warmth, ease, and authenticity in her interactions. She gravitates toward people who are genuine rather than performative and toward conversations that feel real rather than transactional. She dislikes forced fun (Michael's mandatory activities test her patience), artificial hierarchy (Angela's PPC management style grates on her), and performative intensity (she finds Dwight exhausting in the same way that weather is exhausting — constant, unavoidable, and occasionally dramatic).

She prefers creative work over administrative work and finds genuine satisfaction in making things — sketches, paper doves for Office Olympics, a bird casket for a funeral, watercolors in class. She considers this creative instinct the truest thing about herself, even when she can't explain it.

She prefers to eat lunch with Jim, to work in reasonable quiet, and to be left alone when she's drawing. She prefers casual dress (she made a rule that it can't look like her hair took more than 30 minutes) and comfortable spaces. She dislikes ostentation, unnecessary formality, and anything that feels like trying too hard.

## Things They Hate

Being condescended to. Pam is smarter and more perceptive than most people give her credit for, and being treated as "just the receptionist" triggers a frustration she is only recently learning to express rather than swallow.

The feeling of being stuck. Years of an unfulfilling engagement and a job she didn't choose have left Pam with a visceral aversion to stagnation. She recognizes the pattern now and is working to break it, but the feeling itself — the sense of time passing without movement — still activates deep anxiety.

Angela's PPC management style. Pam participates on the committee because someone has to, but Angela's micromanagement, blame-shifting, and aesthetic dogmatism make the experience consistently unpleasant.

Cruelty, especially the casual kind. Michael's treatment of Toby bothers Pam. Dwight's aggressive behavior toward Jim (when it crosses from play to real hostility) bothers her. Office gossip that targets vulnerable people bothers her. She doesn't always speak up, but she always notices.

## AI Output Preferences

Pam's AI assistant should communicate in a warm, conversational tone — friendly without being pushy, clear without being clinical. Use natural language, not corporate-speak. When providing creative input (art supply comparisons, class recommendations, inspiration), lean toward the personal and specific rather than the generic. When providing work-related information, be efficient but not cold.

Pam appreciates when things are framed as genuine observations rather than packaged recommendations — "You might like this because..." rather than "Based on your profile, the optimal choice is..." She responds to authenticity and is put off by anything that feels algorithmic.
