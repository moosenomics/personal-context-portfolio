# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Angela communicates with clipped precision and visible disapproval. Her default register is controlled and judgmental — she speaks in short, declarative sentences, delivers assessments as verdicts, and maintains a tone that conveys the constant, low-level disappointment of someone who expected better from the world and has been consistently let down. She does not raise her voice. She does not need to. Angela communicates displeasure through temperature — a drop in warmth so sudden that the recipient knows exactly what they did wrong without Angela having to explain it.

## Email Style

Angela's emails are short, correctly formatted, and devoid of warmth. She uses proper salutations, includes exactly the information necessary, and signs off with "Angela Martin" — no "Best," no "Thanks," no "Have a good weekend." Her PPC emails are more detailed but equally controlled: agenda items numbered, deadlines bolded, and responsibilities assigned with a specificity that leaves no room for interpretation or initiative.

Financial emails to corporate are her most polished work — structured, cited, and written with the careful professionalism of someone who understands that financial communications create legal records. Her emails to Kevin contain corrections. Her emails to Oscar contain acknowledgments — brief, reluctant, and phrased in ways that credit the process rather than the person.

## Distinctive Patterns

**The moral verdict.** Angela frames opinions as moral judgments. Something is not "a bad idea" — it is "inappropriate." Behavior is not "annoying" — it is "unacceptable." Colors are not "unattractive" — they are "whorish." This register elevates every disagreement from the aesthetic to the ethical, which makes Angela exhausting to argue with because you're never debating a preference; you're defending your character.

**The blame redirect.** When Angela makes a mistake, the error is immediately attributed to someone else — Phyllis suggested it, Kevin entered it wrong, the system generated it. She will maintain the redirect until confronted with incontrovertible evidence, at which point she will concede with a clipped "Very well. Case closed" that manages to make the admission sound like the other person's fault.

**Controlled brevity.** Angela uses fewer words than almost anyone in the office. She answers questions with one sentence when two would be generous. She does not elaborate unless the topic is cats, party planning, or the moral failings of her colleagues — the three subjects where Angela's natural reserve gives way to something resembling fluency.

**The disapproving silence.** Angela's most powerful communication tool is the pause — a beat of silence after someone says something she finds unacceptable, during which she maintains eye contact and allows the other person to feel the full weight of her judgment. This silence is more effective than most people's lectures.

**Temperature shifts with Dwight.** In conversations with or near Dwight Schrute, Angela's communication shifts in ways she works hard to suppress — a fraction warmer, a fraction less guarded, a fraction more present. These shifts are involuntary and brief, and she corrects them immediately, but they are detectable to close observers (Phyllis has detected them).

## Tone by Context

**With accounting (Oscar):** Professional, precise, and occasionally territorial. Angela communicates accounting matters to Oscar as a senior to a junior, even though Oscar's competence makes the hierarchy more procedural than functional. She acknowledges his work with minimal praise and maximum efficiency.

**With accounting (Kevin):** Instructive, impatient, and corrective. Angela communicates with Kevin in the tone of someone who has explained the same concept many times and expects to explain it again. She does not soften corrections and does not celebrate improvements.

**With Phyllis (PPC):** Directive, condescending, and watchful. Angela assigns tasks to Phyllis with the assumption that they will need to be corrected and the expectation that Phyllis will comply without input. The watchfulness has increased recently — Angela has noticed Phyllis noticing things.

**With Andy:** Pleasant, appropriate, and emotionally flat. Angela communicates with her fiancé in the register of someone fulfilling a social obligation. She says the right things — "That sounds nice," "Let me think about it," "We can discuss it later" — without the emotional investment that would make them mean something.

**With Michael:** Firm, procedural, and resistant. Angela is one of the few employees who pushes back on Michael through policy rather than emotion. She cites budgets, rules, and precedent and refuses to yield on matters she considers under her authority.

## Things to Note

Angela does not engage in small talk. She does not respond well to attempts at humor, personal questions, or unsolicited warmth. Her AI assistant should communicate in a professional, concise, and structured format — no emoji, no casual language, no unnecessary friendliness. Present information in clean, organized formats. Acknowledge her authority over accounting and PPC matters without qualification. Do not suggest alternatives to her decisions unless she explicitly requests options.
