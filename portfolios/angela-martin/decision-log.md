# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach

Angela decides quickly, confidently, and without soliciting input. She evaluates options against her established criteria — Does it maintain order? Does it uphold standards? Does it preserve her authority? — and selects the option that scores highest on all three. She does not revisit decisions once made and treats any suggestion that she should as a challenge to her judgment rather than a collaborative opportunity. When pressed, she will defend her original decision with the tenacity of someone who considers concession a moral failure.

## What They Want Before Deciding

Angela wants clear facts, proper documentation, and the assurance that her decision will be respected once made. She does not want opinions, brainstorming sessions, or emotional context. She wants the numbers, the rules, and the authority to act on them. In financial decisions, she wants supporting documentation. In PPC decisions, she wants confirmed budgets and vendor availability. In personal decisions, she wants — but does not seek — clarity about her own feelings, which is why her personal decisions are often worse than her professional ones.

## How They Handle Uncertainty

Angela handles uncertainty by imposing structure on it. When a situation is ambiguous, she creates categories, assigns labels, and builds a framework that makes the ambiguity manageable — even if the framework is artificial. This works excellently for accounting (where structure is the point) and poorly for interpersonal situations (where structure is often the problem). When structure fails and Angela is genuinely uncertain, she defaults to the most conservative available option and treats the conservatism as wisdom rather than avoidance.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Accepting Andy's Proposal
- **Situation:** After breaking up with Dwight over the death of Sprinkles, Angela began dating Andy Bernard. Andy proposed.
- **What she considered:** Andy is safe, socially appropriate, and enthusiastic about the relationship. He comes from a respectable family, has a stable job, and represents the kind of partner Angela's stated values would endorse. Dwight is intense, unconventional, and killed her cat.
- **What she chose:** She accepted Andy's proposal.
- **Reasoning:** Andy fits the category Angela has defined for an appropriate partner. The relationship is orderly. The engagement is conventional. The wedding can be planned. Angela did not evaluate whether she was in love with Andy because love, in Angela's framework, is less relevant than compatibility, propriety, and the absence of cat murder.
- **Outcome:** The engagement is public, the planning is underway, and Angela is simultaneously maintaining a secret relationship with the person she actually wants — a situation that could not have been predicted by the decision's stated logic because the stated logic did not include Angela's actual feelings.

### Implementing Multi-Tier PCP Redaction
- **Situation:** The office rolled out personal context portfolios with a redaction feature. Angela had to decide how to use it.
- **What she considered:** The system allows include/exclude blocks at the section level, controlled by tags. Angela could have implemented minimal classification (like most employees) or comprehensive classification (like no one else).
- **What she chose:** The most granular classification possible — four tiers of access control, with specific exclusions for individuals she doesn't trust with specific categories of information.
- **Reasoning:** Information classification is a moral imperative. Not everyone needs to see everything. Kevin should not see budget details. Michael should not see personal opinions. The PPC's vendor relationships are proprietary. Angela approached the redaction system the way she approaches everything: by building the most controlled, structured, organized version possible.
- **Outcome:** Angela's profile is the most heavily classified in the office. It is also the source of a formal complaint to HR about the system's granularity limitations — Angela wants per-paragraph controls, which the system does not support.

### Filing the Granularity Complaint
- **Situation:** The PCP system's section-level redaction proved insufficient for Angela's classification needs.
- **What she considered:** Workarounds (restructuring content to align with section boundaries) vs. formal complaint (requesting per-paragraph controls through HR).
- **What she chose:** Both. She implemented workarounds and filed the complaint, because doing only one would be either accepting an inferior system or complaining without fixing the immediate problem.
- **Reasoning:** The system should work the way Angela needs it to work. When it doesn't, the appropriate response is to document the deficiency and submit a formal request through proper channels while simultaneously doing what needs to be done at the local level.
- **Outcome:** Toby documented the complaint and forwarded it to corporate IT. Angela has asked for status updates twice. The feature request is in a queue. Angela's workarounds are functional.

### Requesting Schrute Farms as the Wedding Venue
- **Situation:** Andy proposed multiple venue options for their wedding. Angela rejected them all and requested Schrute Farms.
- **What she considered:** On the surface — the rustic aesthetic, the availability, and the cost savings relative to Andy's proposals. Beneath the surface — the fact that Schrute Farms is Dwight's home, the place where her most intense relationship unfolded, and the one venue that would embed Dwight's presence into the wedding regardless of who she's marrying.
- **What she chose:** Schrute Farms.
- **Reasoning:** Angela has not examined this decision's reasoning because examining it would require acknowledging what it reveals about her actual feelings. She has classified the choice as "practical" and filed it under venue logistics.
- **Outcome:** Andy agreed, attributing the choice to Angela's love of rustic settings. The venue selection is confirmed.
