# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach
Stanley makes decisions quickly, with minimal deliberation, based on a single criterion: which option requires the least additional effort and involvement while preserving the status quo. This is not laziness — it's a fully articulated strategy for surviving a work environment he did not design and cannot control. When a decision genuinely matters (his daughter's welfare, his financial security, his health), Stanley is thorough and practical. When a decision involves office activities, he defaults to "no" and considers the matter settled.

## What They Want Before Deciding
Stanley wants to know what the decision will cost him in time and energy. He does not need input from others, does not seek consensus, and does not agonize over options. If the information is available, he decides immediately. If it isn't, he waits — not anxiously, but with the patience of someone who has learned that most urgent-seeming decisions turn out not to matter.

## How They Handle Uncertainty
Stanley's response to uncertainty is inaction. If he doesn't know the answer, he does not guess, does not brainstorm, and does not explore. He waits for the situation to resolve itself or for someone else to resolve it. This strategy works more often than organizational theory would predict, because most office uncertainty is manufactured by people (Michael) who create problems that would not exist without their intervention.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### The "Did I Stutter?" Confrontation
- **Situation:** Michael called a meeting to discuss "energizing" the office. Stanley was doing his crossword. Michael demanded Stanley put it down and contribute. Stanley said "Leave me alone, dammit." Michael persisted.
- **What he considered:** Whether the paycheck was worth this specific moment of indignity.
- **What he chose:** Direct, public confrontation. "Did I stutter?" — delivered with enough force to silence the entire conference room.
- **Reasoning:** Stanley's boundary had been crossed. The crossword is not a hobby; it is the mechanism by which Stanley tolerates the meeting. Removing it removed his coping strategy in real time. The response was not calculated — it was the circuit breaker tripping.
- **Outcome:** Toby intervened. Michael attempted to address the insubordination. Stanley and Michael eventually reached an understanding: Stanley does not respect Michael, this will not change, but Stanley will remain civil. Both parties have largely honored this agreement, though "civil" remains a generous description of Stanley's baseline demeanor toward Michael.

### Refusing the Utica Transfer
- **Situation:** Karen Filippelli, now managing the Utica branch, attempted to recruit Stanley with promises of better pay and a fresh start. Michael, Dwight, and Jim traveled to Utica to "save" Stanley.
- **What he considered:** Higher salary at Utica versus the cost of disrupting his routine — new commute, new desk, new coworkers, new manager who might be even worse than Michael (unlikely but possible).
- **What he chose:** Stayed at Scranton.
- **Reasoning:** Stanley's decision was not about loyalty to Michael or attachment to his coworkers. It was about the devil he knows. Scranton is dysfunctional, but it's a dysfunction he has mapped and optimized around. Starting over at Utica would mean rebuilding his defensive perimeter from scratch. The raise wasn't enough to justify the disruption.
- **Outcome:** Stanley remained at his desk, across from Phyllis, crossword in hand. Michael took credit for retaining him. Stanley did not correct this.

### Minimal Profile Engagement
- **Situation:** Corporate mandated personal context portfolios served via MCP. Every employee was asked to complete their profile.
- **What he considered:** The minimum amount of information necessary to satisfy the requirement without inviting interaction, follow-up, or interest from anyone.
- **What he chose:** Stanley filled out the bare essentials — name, title, basic responsibilities — and left every optional section blank or one-sentence. He added exclude tags for michael-scott and dwight-schrute on the few sections that contained anything beyond job function, not because those sections were particularly revealing but on principle: if the system allows you to hide things from management, you should hide things from management.
- **Reasoning:** "If I write more, they'll read more. If they read more, they'll talk to me more. If they talk to me more, I'll need a new crossword book sooner."
- **Outcome:** Stanley's profile is the shortest in the office. His AI assistant has very little to work with and consequently sends very short, direct emails — which is, unintentionally, the most accurate voice-matching in the entire branch. Stanley has noticed that Michael stopped ambushing him with personal details from his profile, because there are no personal details in his profile. He considers this a complete validation of his approach.

### Not Reporting What He's Noticed
- **Situation:** Stanley has noticed irregularities in the profile system — specifically, people seeming to know things about each other that weren't public, and Michael referencing a "succession planning initiative" that Stanley is fairly certain Michael didn't create. He has also noticed that Dwight's profile apparently lists interests Dwight has never mentioned, and that people have been asking Dwight about dolphins.
- **What he considered:** Whether to say something to Toby, to Michael, or to anyone.
- **What he chose:** Nothing. Stanley chose to do nothing.
- **Reasoning:** Whatever is happening with the profile system is not Stanley's problem. It does not affect his clients, his paycheck, or his crossword. If someone is tampering with profiles, that's a problem for HR, IT, or whoever built the system. Stanley did not ask for this system. He filled out the minimum. He is not going to investigate, report, or involve himself in whatever soap opera is playing out in the profile data. He will continue to do his crossword, sell his paper, and leave at 5 PM.
- **Outcome:** Stanley knows more than he's saying and cares less than anyone would expect. This is his default state.
