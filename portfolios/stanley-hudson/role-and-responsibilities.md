# Role & Responsibilities

**Title:** Sales Representative
**Organization:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton
**Reports to:** Michael Scott (Regional Manager)
**Direct Reports:** None

## Core Responsibilities
Stanley sells paper and paper products to an established portfolio of regional business accounts. His responsibilities include client retention, order fulfillment coordination, occasional prospecting, and quarterly sales target attainment. He does all of this adequately and none of it with enthusiasm. His client relationships are long-standing and low-maintenance — Stanley's customers stay because he's reliable, doesn't oversell them, and doesn't waste their time with unnecessary check-ins. This is the same quality his coworkers would identify as "not caring," but his clients call it professionalism.

## Reporting Structure
Reports to Michael Scott, who Stanley regards as an obstacle between himself and a quiet workday. Lateral peers include Dwight Schrute, Jim Halpert, Andy Bernard, and Phyllis Vance. Stanley has no interest in the internal status competitions that consume Dwight and Andy. He does not want to be Michael's number-two. He does not want to be anyone's number anything. He wants to be left alone to do his crossword puzzle until a client calls.

## Typical Week
Stanley arrives at the office on time — not early, not late. He sits at his desk, opens his crossword book, and begins his day. When the phone rings, he answers it. When a client needs something, he handles it. When Michael calls a meeting, Stanley attends with his crossword puzzle and a facial expression that communicates his feelings about the meeting more clearly than any words could. He takes lunch at his desk or with Phyllis. He does not stay past 5 PM under any circumstances — the building could be on fire (and has been, sort of) and Stanley would still be heading to the parking lot at 5:01.

Client calls happen throughout the day, but Stanley does not cold-call. His pipeline is mature and self-sustaining. New business comes from referrals within his existing network, not from initiative. Field visits happen rarely and only when a client specifically requests an in-person meeting, which Stanley will accommodate but not enjoy.

The rhythm of Stanley's week is defined by what he avoids: voluntary participation, extra projects, conversations about feelings, anything Michael calls "fun," and any meeting that could have been an email — which, in Stanley's view, is every meeting.
