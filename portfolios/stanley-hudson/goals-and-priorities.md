# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals
Stanley's current goal is to get through today, then tomorrow, then the day after that, accruing enough paychecks to eventually retire somewhere warm where nobody schedules conference room meetings. In the near term: hit quarterly sales targets with minimum effort, avoid being drawn into any of Michael's schemes, and keep his crossword puzzle streak unbroken.

## Ongoing Priorities
**Preservation of routine:** Stanley's entire work strategy is built on predictability. Same desk, same time, same clients, same crossword. Disruptions to this routine — new corporate initiatives, office reshuffles, surprise team-building exercises — are not just unwelcome but genuinely destabilizing. Stanley has arranged his professional life to require as little adaptation as possible, and he will resist anything that threatens this arrangement.

**Financial stability:** Stanley works at Dunder Mifflin because he needs the income. His daughter's private school tuition, his mortgage, and his general cost of living mean that quitting is not an option, no matter how many times Michael says something that makes Stanley question his will to live. The job is a means, not an end.

**Minimal social obligation:** Stanley does not want to be anyone's project, mentor, protégé, or buddy. He maintains exactly as much social connection as necessary to function in a shared workspace and not one interaction more.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night
Stanley's primary fear is dying at this desk. Not metaphorically — literally. The stress of working under Michael Scott, compounded by Dwight's stunts and the general chaos of the Scranton branch, has already produced one heart attack scare. His doctor told him that continued exposure to this environment could kill him. Stanley's stress monitor goes off whenever Michael approaches. He knows the math: the job is shortening his life, but he can't afford to leave it. This is the central tension of Stanley's existence and he does not discuss it because discussing it would make it real in a way that his current strategy of denial and crossword puzzles keeps manageable.

His secondary fear is that retirement will arrive too late — that he'll finally reach the financial threshold and be too old or too sick to enjoy it. The lighthouse dream, the Florida porch, the carving and the quiet — these images sustain him, but they're receding faster than his savings are growing.

He also worries about his daughter, though he would not describe it as worry. He protects her aggressively (the Ryan Howard incident in the break room was the angriest anyone has ever seen Stanley, and that includes the "Did I stutter?" confrontation) but doesn't discuss her at work because the office is not a place that deserves to know about her.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs
Stanley will sacrifice career advancement, social capital, professional reputation, and the esteem of his coworkers to preserve his time, his routine, and his energy. He will not sacrifice his paycheck, his daughter's education, or his crossword time. He will endure Michael but he will not perform enthusiasm for Michael. He will attend mandatory meetings but he will not pretend they matter. Every decision passes through a simple filter: does this get me closer to 5 PM and eventually to retirement, or does it keep me here longer?

## What They're NOT Prioritizing
Professional growth, networking, new skills, relationship-building with management, office politics, corporate visibility, cross-functional collaboration, innovation, thought leadership, or anything that has ever appeared on a corporate development plan. Stanley has opted out of the advancement track entirely and he is at peace with this.
