# Tools & Systems

## Primary Tools
Stanley uses a standard-issue Dell desktop computer, a desk phone, and a crossword puzzle book. Of these three, the crossword puzzle book sees the most consistent daily use. His computer runs Outlook for email and the Dunder Mifflin CRM for order tracking, both of which Stanley uses with mechanical competence and zero curiosity about features he hasn't needed in the last fifteen years. His desk phone is his primary sales tool — he picks it up when it rings, he occasionally dials out to check on an order, and he hangs up when the conversation is over. He does not use a Blackberry, does not want a Blackberry, and will not acknowledge that other people's Blackberries are ringing.

Stanley's personal context portfolio is served via MCP to his AI assistant. He filled it out with the minimum viable information and has not touched it since. His profile is the shortest in the office. He did not include personal interests, detailed communication preferences, or anything that might invite interaction. When the AI assistant surfaces information from other people's profiles, Stanley ignores it. He has never asked his AI a question about a coworker and does not intend to start.

## Key Workflows
A client calls. Stanley checks inventory and pricing. Stanley processes the order. Stanley moves on. This workflow has not changed in years and Stanley sees no reason for it to change. Internal communication happens when absolutely necessary — a reply-all to an email Michael should not have sent, a brief exchange with Phyllis about a shared account, a one-word response to a question from accounting. Everything else is filtered out.

## Current System Issues
Stanley's primary system issue is that the profile system exists at all. He considers it an invasion of privacy, a waste of time, and further evidence that corporate makes decisions without consulting the people who actually do the work. He has not filed a complaint because filing a complaint would require engaging with the system, which would defeat the purpose of ignoring it. He did notice that Michael began referencing personal details from other people's profiles in meetings, which confirmed everything Stanley already believed about giving management access to personal information.
