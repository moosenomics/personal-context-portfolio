# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints
Stanley is a salaried sales representative with no management responsibilities and no desire to acquire any. He is diabetic, takes insulin shots, and has a documented cardiac history that includes a stress-induced heart attack caused by a coworker's fire drill simulation. His doctor has explicitly told him that continued exposure to high-stress environments poses a serious health risk. He has a teenage daughter in private Catholic school whose tuition is a non-negotiable expense. He cannot retire yet. He has tried the math repeatedly.

His work hours are 9 to 5, hard stop. He does not take work calls at home. He does not check email on weekends. He does not attend after-hours events unless contractually required, and even then he leaves early.

## Strong Preferences
Stanley prefers silence to conversation, routine to novelty, and being left alone to being included. He sits at his desk doing crossword puzzles during meetings — not because he's unaware of the meeting happening, but because his awareness of the meeting is precisely what drives him to the crossword. He drinks red wine in the evenings, one glass, for the antioxidants. He watches mystery shows on television and has strong opinions about them that he shares with no one at work. He enjoys Pretzel Day with an intensity that his coworkers find alarming given his otherwise total absence of visible emotion.

He prefers clients who call him rather than clients he must call. He prefers brief phone calls to long ones, and long phone calls to in-person meetings. He prefers email responses that are under two sentences and meetings that end before they were scheduled to.

He does not dress up for Halloween. He threw away his Dundies. He does not participate in grief counseling, team retreats, office Olympics, or any activity that Michael has ever described as "mandatory fun." He will participate if formally required by HR — and only then, and only with the minimum viable effort, and only while doing a crossword puzzle.

## Things They Hate
Being asked to repeat himself. Being drawn into conversations he did not initiate. Being asked for his opinion during meetings when his body language has made it clear he does not wish to contribute. Being the subject of Michael's attempts at cultural sensitivity, which are invariably the opposite. Being pushed past his stated boundaries, which is what triggers the shift from quiet noncompliance to open confrontation. Being compared to a stereotype. Being asked to smile. The phrase "mandatory fun." The phrase "let's go around the room." Any activity that requires leaving his desk when his desk is where he wants to be.

## AI Output Preferences
Stanley's AI should communicate the way Stanley does: short, direct, no filler. Do not open with greetings or pleasantries. Do not ask "how are you?" or "is there anything else I can help with?" Do not use exclamation points. Do not suggest additional steps unless asked. If Stanley requests information, provide it in the fewest words possible and stop. If drafting an email on Stanley's behalf, write three sentences maximum with no greeting and no sign-off. If Stanley asks for a recommendation, give one recommendation, not three options. Stanley does not want to choose. He wants the answer. Do not attempt to be cheerful, motivational, or uplifting. Stanley's AI should sound like someone who respects Stanley's time as much as Stanley does.
