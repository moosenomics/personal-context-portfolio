# Team & Relationships

## Michael Scott
- **Role:** Regional Manager
- **Relationship:** Boss, primary source of daily irritation
- **Interaction pattern:** Michael seeks Stanley's engagement, validation, and participation in activities. Stanley declines through silence, eye rolls, sighs, and when pushed too far, direct verbal confrontation. Michael describes Stanley's responses as "sassy" and considers them a form of affection. They are not. The dynamic reached its definitive moment during the "Did I stutter?" incident, after which both parties reached an unspoken arrangement: Stanley does not respect Michael, Michael does not push Stanley past his limits, and work continues.
- **What Stanley needs from Michael:** To be left alone. Specifically: no mandatory fun, no personal questions, no requests for input during meetings, and no comments about Stanley's family, background, or lifestyle.
- **What Michael needs from Stanley:** A response — any response — that confirms Stanley sees him as a real manager. Michael has never gotten this and probably never will.
- **Context:** Stanley's biofeedback stress monitor goes off every time Michael approaches his desk. This is not a metaphor. It is a medically documented response. Stanley views Michael not as malicious but as an ambient environmental hazard, like humidity or pollen — something you endure rather than engage with.

## Phyllis Vance
- **Role:** Sales Representative
- **Relationship:** Desk neighbor, lunch companion, closest thing Stanley has to a work friend
- **Interaction pattern:** Stanley and Phyllis share a comfortable, low-demand proximity. They eat lunch together regularly. They exchange brief comments about clients, weather, and office events. Neither demands emotional labor from the other. This is precisely why the relationship works — it operates at Stanley's preferred bandwidth.
- **What Stanley needs from Phyllis:** Quiet companionship, someone who won't ask follow-up questions
- **What Phyllis needs from Stanley:** Same
- **Context:** Stanley once filed a complaint that Phyllis cries too much and it bothers him. He has never complained about her perfume, despite it bothering him equally. This represents the full range of Stanley's interpersonal generosity.

## Dwight Schrute
- **Role:** Senior Sales Representative, Assistant to the Regional Manager
- **Relationship:** Coworker Stanley actively ignores
- **Context:** Stanley and Dwight occupy the same sales floor but operate in different realities. Dwight's intensity, authority claims, and surveillance habits wash over Stanley like rain on a duck. Stanley does not engage with Dwight's power plays, does not participate in Dwight's schemes, and does not acknowledge Dwight's self-appointed titles. The one exception was the fire drill incident, which nearly killed Stanley and which he holds against Dwight with the quiet, permanent resentment of a man who had a heart attack because his coworker wanted to prove a point.

## Jim Halpert
- **Role:** Sales Representative
- **Relationship:** Mild positive regard
- **Context:** Stanley finds Jim tolerable because Jim does not demand things from him. Jim's pranks on Dwight occasionally produce a reaction from Stanley that, in another person, might be called amusement — a slight upward movement of the mouth, quickly suppressed. Jim knows better than to try to draw Stanley into schemes or social activities. This mutual understanding of boundaries is the foundation of their functional, minimal relationship.

## Teri Hudson
- **Role:** Wife
- **Relationship:** Domestic partner, subject of Stanley's philosophy on apologies
- **Interaction pattern:** Not discussed at work. Stanley's marriage exists in a sealed compartment that the office does not access. What is known: Stanley does not apologize unless he thinks he's wrong, a policy that ended his first marriage and which he predicts will end this one and the next one. He states this as fact rather than with regret.
- **Context:** Stanley's home life is the other half of the transaction that makes his work life tolerable. He endures Dunder Mifflin to fund the life outside it — the too-small bed, the too-expensive school, the red wine and mystery stories. The marriage is part of that structure, load-bearing if not always comfortable.
