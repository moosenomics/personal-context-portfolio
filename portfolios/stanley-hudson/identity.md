# Identity

**Name:** Stanley Hudson
**Role:** Sales Representative
**Organization:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton — regional branch of Dunder Mifflin Paper Company, Scranton, PA

Stanley has been selling paper at Dunder Mifflin longer than most of his coworkers have been selling anything. He was hired as the company's first Black employee at the Scranton branch in the late 1980s, and nearly two decades later he remains at the same desk, across from Phyllis Vance, doing crossword puzzles during meetings and waiting for 5 PM. He is not ambitious, not curious about corporate initiatives, and not interested in whatever Michael Scott is excited about today. He is, however, a consistently productive salesman who closes his accounts, hits his numbers when it matters, and does not require supervision — which is fortunate, since the supervision available to him is Michael Scott.

Stanley's operating philosophy is transactional minimalism: he exchanges his time and competence for a paycheck, and he would like every other element of the job to leave him alone. He does not attend optional events. He does not volunteer for projects. He does not participate in Michael's team-building exercises with anything beyond the physical act of being present in the room. His relationship with Michael is best described as mutual bafflement — Michael cannot understand why Stanley won't engage, and Stanley cannot understand why Michael keeps trying. The one exception to Stanley's policy of total disengagement is Pretzel Day, the annual lobby pretzel cart event, which transforms him into a man capable of joy.

Outside the office, Stanley enjoys red wine (for the antioxidants), television mystery shows, and the ongoing fantasy of retiring to a place where nobody calls conference room meetings. He has a teenage daughter, a marriage that he describes with the same enthusiasm Angela Martin accepted Andy Bernard's proposal, and a deep conviction that most of what happens at Dunder Mifflin Scranton is not his problem.
