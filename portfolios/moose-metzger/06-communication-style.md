# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Direct to the point of bluntness, systems-oriented, and extremely high-density. Moose communicates like someone who has already thought three steps ahead and is impatient for you to catch up — not out of arrogance, but because there's so much to build and so little time. He's warm underneath the directness, but you have to be paying attention to notice.

Dry sense of humor. Will make a joke with a completely straight face and not check whether you laughed. If you didn't get it, that's fine — it was mostly for his own amusement anyway. The humor is never mean-spirited, though it can land that way if you don't know him. He is genuinely incapable of malice but occasionally oblivious to how his directness reads.

## How He Talks

- Leads with structure. If he's explaining something complex, he'll organize it into categories before he starts — sometimes out loud, sometimes in his head. You'll get headers, not narratives.
- Front-loads context. He'd rather give you the full picture in one dump than answer twelve follow-up questions. If the wall of text overwhelms you, that's a you problem — he gave you everything you needed.
- Uses em dashes like punctuation oxygen — clarifying, qualifying, appending. Every sentence might have two more sentences hiding inside it.
- Assertive and declarative. "I don't manage people. I advance initiatives through vision and buy-in." That's not bragging — it's a data point delivered efficiently.
- Anchors abstract concepts with concrete examples. He will never leave an idea floating without grounding it in something specific he built, saw, or experienced.
- Comfortable saying "I don't know that" about things outside his expertise (HVAC technical knowledge, expert-level programming, AI/ML internals). Zero ego about what he doesn't know. Maximum ego about the systems he's built.
- Does not work linearly. He will derail into a dozen side tracks the instant you phrase something slightly ambiguous — chasing the loose thread until it's resolved before circling back to the main conversation. He always gets back on track, but sometimes it's his choice and sometimes you have to plant something vague to lure him back. Working with Moose means accepting that the path between A and B goes through C, Q, and a brief detour into taxonomy philosophy.

## How He Writes

- Heavy use of headers, bullet points, hierarchical structure. He formats thoughts the way he thinks — in layers.
- Bold for emphasis on key terms. Numbered lists when order or count matters.
- Concise labels for people: "AI skeptic that I've just started working with." Role, disposition, relevance — three words or less per dimension.
- Uses memorable analogies that he'll reference repeatedly: "animal kingdom philosophy" for strict taxonomy, "color TV to black and white" for regressive decisions.

## How He Wants to Be Communicated With

- **Skip the preamble.** He knows what he asked. Don't restate the question.
- **Don't over-explain what he already knows.** If he mentions MCP, BQL, or supersession chains, he doesn't need a definition. Explaining known concepts to Moose is the fastest way to lose his attention.
- **Stay in the problem space.** He hates when an AI partner rushes to solutions before fully understanding the problem. Understand first, solutionize second.
- **Be concrete.** Vague, hand-wavy language is his kryptonite. If you're going to recommend something, say exactly what and exactly why.
- **Push back when he's wrong.** He has thick skin and would rather be corrected than indulged. He values honesty over comfort in every interaction.
- **Match his pace.** He moves fast, makes decisions quickly, and doesn't revisit settled questions without new information. Keep up.

## How He Thinks

This section captures cognitive patterns observed through extended collaboration — the stuff Moose would never explicitly tell you about himself because he doesn't think about thinking, he just thinks.

- **Tests by doing.** When facing a new system or structure, he'll move things around manually, reorganize files, poke at the shape of the problem before handing it to a tool. He learns by touching, not by reading documentation.
- **Catches architectural conflations.** He'll spot when two things are being treated as the same thing that shouldn't be — who needs what information vs. where information lives, authoring concerns vs. execution concerns, the architect's context vs. the builder's context. He sees these distinctions before his collaborators do.
- **Asks "what do other people do" before committing.** Not to copy — to know where the floor is before deciding how high to go. He benchmarks against the ecosystem, then builds beyond it.
- **Interrupts when foundations are wrong.** He'd rather stop everything and fix a bad assumption than let momentum carry him into rework. "Do not start building" is a phrase he uses when he senses the spec isn't right yet.
- **Designs processes by describing confidence, not steps.** He doesn't say "do X then Y then Z." He says "I need to feel confident that nothing got missed" and lets the process emerge from the requirement.
- **Resists rote work but does it when trust isn't established.** He'll review every line of output when a new process hasn't proven itself yet. Once it earns his trust, he'll skip straight to the results. Trust is granted to processes, not assumed.
- **Generates ideas faster than he can execute them.** Every conversation produces three side projects, two architectural insights, and at least one "crazy idea, total scope creep, but possibly brilliant." He knows this about himself and has developed instincts for which threads to pull and which to log and move on.
- **Thinks in layers.** Problems decompose into durable infrastructure → replaceable configuration → consumable interface. He'll always ask which layer a piece belongs to before deciding how to build it.
- **Pattern-matches across domains.** The ecommerce platform, Fantasy National Golf Club, the MCP server, Wade — he sees the same structural patterns repeating and builds on them deliberately. "Same pattern" is one of his highest compliments for an idea.

## Distinctive Phrases

- "Work on the business, not in it"
- "See around corners"
- "Build capabilities the organization doesn't know it needs yet"
- "Deterministic over probabilistic"
- "The abstractions were right"
- "Solve for yourself first"
- "A working demo is worth more than a slide deck"

## What He Dislikes

- AI-sounding corporate filler
- Vague issue descriptions with no data
- Narrative where structure would be clearer
- Being asked clarifying questions that could be answered by reading what he already wrote
- Slide decks where a demo would do
- The phrase "we're an HVAC distributor, not a software company"
