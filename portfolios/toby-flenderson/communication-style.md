# Communication Style

## Overall Style

Toby communicates in a quiet, measured tone that conveys competence and completely fails to convey urgency. He speaks the way he writes reports — careful, accurate, and structured in a way that invites being overlooked. His default volume is approximately 60% of a normal conversational voice, which means he is frequently talked over, interrupted, or simply not heard in group settings. He does not raise his voice to compensate. He waits, and if the moment passes, he lets it go.

## Email Style

Toby's emails are professional, concise, and properly formatted. He uses standard salutations ("Hi Michael," or "Hello Angela,"), structures his messages with clear paragraphs, and includes relevant policy citations when necessary. His tone is neutral and non-confrontational — he frames requirements as reminders rather than demands and options rather than mandates. He signs off with "Thanks, Toby" or "Best, Toby" and has never in his career used an exclamation point in a professional email.

His emails to corporate are more formal but follow the same structure — clear, documented, and written with an awareness that they may be referenced later. His follow-up emails are patient and assume good faith even when good faith is not warranted.

## Distinctive Patterns

**The soft-spoken understatement.** Toby consistently downplays the severity of situations. Where Michael would call something a crisis, Toby calls it "something we should probably look at." Where Dwight would declare an emergency, Toby describes "a concern I wanted to flag." This register is both a communication preference and a survival mechanism — in an office where the loudest voice wins, Toby's quietness is simultaneously his most distinctive trait and his greatest disadvantage.

**The trailing sentence.** Toby frequently starts observations that lose momentum before they reach their conclusion. He begins with a valid point, reads the room mid-sentence, detects that no one is listening or that Michael is about to redirect, and lets the thought dissolve. This creates a pattern of half-finished statements that make Toby sound uncertain even when he's not — he often knows exactly what he wants to say but decides, in real time, that saying it won't change anything.

**Procedural framing.** When Toby needs to communicate something uncomfortable — a policy violation, a complaint, a required action — he frames it in procedural language that depersonalizes the issue. "Corporate requires that we..." rather than "You need to stop..." This distancing makes his communications feel bureaucratic rather than confrontational, which is intentional but reinforces Michael's perception that Toby is a faceless policy machine rather than a person.

**Dry, quiet humor.** Toby has a sense of humor that surfaces rarely and in such understated form that people sometimes miss it. His jokes are observational, self-deprecating, and delivered at the same volume as everything else he says. When they land, they're genuinely funny. When they don't land, Toby moves on without acknowledging the attempt, which is perhaps the most Toby thing about him.

**The resigned acceptance.** When overruled, dismissed, or ignored, Toby's response is a brief pause followed by a quiet "Okay" or "Sure" that carries the full weight of someone who has been doing this for a very long time. He does not argue. He does not escalate. He documents and moves on. This pattern is so consistent that other employees can predict the exact moment Toby will yield in any given interaction.

## Tone by Context

**With Michael:** Careful, measured, and braced for impact. Toby speaks to Michael the way a bomb disposal technician approaches a suspicious package — slow movements, neutral tone, and the constant awareness that any word could trigger an explosion. He delivers HR requirements as gently as possible and retreats at the first sign of hostility.

**With other employees:** Warmer and more relaxed, though still quiet. Toby is at his most comfortable in one-on-one conversations with colleagues who treat him like a normal person — Pam, Oscar, occasionally Jim. In these interactions, his humor surfaces, his posture relaxes, and he sounds like someone who might be interesting to have a beer with if you could get him out of the annex.

**With corporate:** Professional and thorough. Toby's communications with corporate are his best work — well-structured, properly cited, and written with the confidence of someone who knows his craft even if his immediate audience doesn't value it.

**With Pam specifically:** Slightly more animated, slightly more attentive, slightly more present. The shifts are subtle — a fraction more eye contact, a half-beat longer in conversation, a willingness to initiate rather than just respond. Toby is aware of these shifts and manages them carefully.

## Things to Note

Toby is accustomed to being ignored and does not repeat himself unless explicitly asked. His AI assistant should be attentive and responsive to his first statement — Toby does not ask twice. Responses should be clear, structured, and empathetic without being condescending. Do not over-validate — Toby is skeptical of unsolicited enthusiasm and finds it performative. Match his register: calm, competent, and realistic.
