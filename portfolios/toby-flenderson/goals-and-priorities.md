# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals

Getting the profile system security report read and acted upon. Toby knows this is unlikely but considers it his professional obligation to have produced the report and to continue following up until someone with authority acknowledges it. He is weighing whether to escalate directly to corporate IT, which would be procedurally correct but would further deteriorate his already non-functional relationship with Michael.

Being a good father to Sasha. Toby's custody arrangement means his time with his daughter is structured and limited, and he is deliberate about being present when he has her. This is not something he discusses at work, because work is not a place where Toby shares things.

Finishing his novel. This is a long-horizon goal that Toby pursues in increments too small to constitute momentum but too consistent to call abandoned. He writes a paragraph here, a sentence there, and carries the story forward with the same patient persistence he brings to everything else.

## Ongoing Priorities

Keeping the Scranton branch compliant. This is Toby's core professional priority and the one that consumes most of his working hours. It is thankless, invisible work that prevents legal and regulatory consequences the office would never know to attribute to his absence.

Maintaining institutional equilibrium. Toby has learned, through years of experience, that the Scranton branch operates on a fragile balance of personalities, grudges, and alliances. His role is to absorb shocks, document risks, and intervene only when something threatens to cross from "dysfunctional but functional" to "actually dangerous." He picks his battles with an economy born of losing most of them.

Preserving his own sanity. This is not a goal Toby would articulate, but it manifests in the small structures he maintains — his filing system, his novel, his quiet conversations with Pam, his competent work on the investigation. These are the things that remind Toby he is good at something, even if the people around him don't notice.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night

Irrelevance. Toby's deepest fear is that he has spent his career doing work that genuinely doesn't matter — that the branch would be exactly the same without him and that the compliance infrastructure he maintains is a bureaucratic formality rather than a meaningful contribution. The profile investigation has sharpened this fear: he has done excellent work, and it has had zero impact.

Ending up alone. Toby's divorce, his unrequited feelings for Pam, and his general difficulty forming close connections all feed a quiet anxiety about the trajectory of his personal life. He does not dwell on this publicly, but it surfaces in his wistful moments and in the careful way he manages his time with Sasha.

The possibility that his novel is not good. Toby has invested years of marginal effort into a creative project that he has never shown anyone. The possibility that it doesn't work — that the story he's been assembling paragraph by paragraph is mediocre — is something he avoids confronting by never putting it in front of a reader.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs

Toby thinks about tradeoffs carefully and then chooses the path of least resistance, which he would describe as "the path of least disruption." He weighs the cost of action against the cost of inaction and almost always concludes that inaction is cheaper. This isn't laziness — it's the calculated restraint of someone who has learned that his interventions are usually overruled and that the energy spent fighting is rarely recovered. When Toby does act, it's because the tradeoff math has shifted — the risk of inaction has become greater than the cost of being ignored.

The one area where Toby doesn't compromise is documentation. He will always write the report, file the complaint, and create the record — even when he knows no one will read it. The record is his integrity made tangible.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing

Self-promotion, visibility, career advancement within Dunder Mifflin, social positioning within the office, or any initiative that requires Michael's enthusiastic support. Toby has also deprioritized his creative ambitions in any timeframe shorter than "eventually" and his romantic life in any timeframe shorter than "when Sasha is older."
