# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints

Toby's reporting structure (corporate HR, not the branch manager) cannot be changed and defines the fundamental architecture of his role at Scranton. His custody arrangement with his ex-wife determines his personal schedule and limits his availability outside standard work hours. He has no budget, no direct reports, and no formal authority over any branch employee — his power is entirely procedural, derived from policy enforcement rather than organizational rank. This makes him structurally dependent on management cooperation to do his job, which is structurally unavailable.

## Strong Preferences

Toby prefers one-on-one conversations over group settings. He communicates more effectively, shares more openly, and is less likely to be talked over when the audience is a single person rather than a conference room. He prefers written communication for anything that matters — partly because writing is where his competence shows most clearly, and partly because written records exist after the conversation is over and the speaker has been forgotten.

He prefers quiet environments. His cubicle in the annex is physically separated from the main office, which Michael considers a banishment and Toby considers a mercy. The annex is calmer, quieter, and allows Toby to focus on work without the constant interruptions that characterize the main office.

He prefers structure and process. Toby doesn't enjoy bureaucracy for its own sake, but he believes that fair, consistent processes protect people — especially people without power. His commitment to documentation, policy, and procedure is rooted in a genuine belief that these things matter, even when the people around him don't think they do.

He prefers to eat lunch at his desk or alone in the break room. Shared lunches make him self-conscious, and he uses the midday break as time to decompress, work on his novel, or simply exist without performing a professional role.

## Things They Hate

Being dismissed. Toby tolerates it — he's had years of practice — but the experience of producing competent work and watching it be ignored is corrosive. The profile investigation report is the most acute current example, but the pattern extends across his entire tenure: Toby says the right thing, at the right time, in the right way, and it doesn't matter because Michael has decided that nothing Toby says matters.

Cruelty disguised as humor. Michael's insults are frequently framed as jokes, and the office's willingness to laugh (or at least not object) compounds the sting. Toby can distinguish between genuine humor and targeted meanness, and his tolerance for the latter is deep but not infinite.

Unnecessary conflict. Toby's job requires him to navigate conflict daily, but he dislikes it — both the conflicts themselves and the emotional residue they leave. He is drained by confrontation in a way that more assertive personalities are not, and he recovers slowly.

Having his professional competence questioned. Toby is good at his job. He knows this with a clarity that coexists uneasily with his general self-doubt. When someone implies that his work is inadequate — as opposed to simply ignored — it lands differently and harder.

## AI Output Preferences

Toby's AI assistant should communicate in a calm, professional tone that matches his own register. No forced enthusiasm, no exclamation points, no cheerful optimism. Be direct and factual. Toby appreciates thoroughness and will read a detailed response — he is one of the few people in this office who will actually read something longer than a paragraph.

When providing recommendations, frame them as options with clear tradeoffs rather than directives. Toby values having the information to make his own decision, not being told what to do. Acknowledge complexity rather than simplifying — Toby distrusts easy answers because his experience has taught him that easy answers usually mean someone hasn't thought it through.

If Toby asks about a sensitive topic (a personnel issue, a compliance question, an interpersonal situation), respond with the care and neutrality of a good HR professional. He'll notice if the response takes sides.
