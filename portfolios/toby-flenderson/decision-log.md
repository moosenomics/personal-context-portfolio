# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach

Toby decides carefully, acts quietly, and accepts the outcome with minimal visible emotion. He gathers information, weighs options, considers the likely reactions of the people involved, and then chooses the path that minimizes disruption while preserving his professional integrity. His decisions tend to be correct and timely — the problem is not his judgment but his inability to ensure his decisions are implemented by the people with actual authority.

## What They Want Before Deciding

Toby wants documentation — evidence, precedent, policy citations. He wants to understand the procedural landscape before he acts, because procedure is the only authority he has. He also wants to assess the emotional temperature of the people involved — not because he'll avoid doing the right thing, but because he'll adjust his approach to minimize the blast radius. He does not need validation or approval, but he does need to feel that his decision is defensible if questioned later.

## How They Handle Uncertainty

Toby handles uncertainty by documenting what he knows and flagging what he doesn't. He is comfortable with ambiguity — years in HR have taught him that most situations are more complicated than they appear and that premature certainty causes more problems than patient investigation. When genuinely uncertain, Toby slows down rather than speeding up, which is the opposite of Michael's approach and one of the many reasons they are incompatible.

In personal matters, Toby handles uncertainty by waiting. He waited to see if his feelings for Pam would resolve. He waits to see if his novel is good enough to share. He waits to see if his career will change. The waiting is not strategic — it's the default behavior of someone who has learned that action often doesn't change outcomes and that patience at least preserves options.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Producing the 11-Page Report
- **Situation:** Michael assigned Toby to investigate the profile system anomalies (Operation Goldenface). Toby had the option to produce a cursory report or a thorough one.
- **What he considered:** The time investment required for a thorough investigation vs. the near certainty that Michael wouldn't read it regardless.
- **What he chose:** Thorough investigation. Toby examined the system's update pipeline, traced modification timestamps, identified the JSON staging authentication gap, and documented the evidence in an 11-page report with recommended remediations.
- **Reasoning:** "If I'm going to do the work, I'm going to do it right." Toby's commitment to professional integrity overrides his awareness that the output will be ignored. The report exists not because anyone will read it but because it should exist — because the vulnerability is real, the evidence is clear, and someone should have documented it properly.
- **Outcome:** The report sits unread on Michael's desk beneath a copy of Variety magazine. Toby has sent two follow-up emails. Both unanswered.

### Not Directly Naming Dwight in the Report
- **Situation:** Toby's investigation pointed clearly at Dwight Schrute as the most likely actor behind the profile modifications. He had to decide whether to name Dwight explicitly.
- **What he considered:** Naming Dwight would transform the report from a security analysis into an accusation. An accusation requires a different level of evidence, a different set of procedures, and — critically — a manager willing to act on it.
- **What he chose:** He documented the evidence (modification timestamps, session patterns, access records) and left the identification to the reader. The evidence is clear enough that any competent reader would reach the conclusion. Toby structured the report to lead there without stating it.
- **Reasoning:** Procedural integrity. An HR investigation should present findings, not conclusions. If Michael reads the report, the evidence speaks for itself. If Michael doesn't read the report — which is what happened — then naming Dwight would have accomplished nothing except creating a paper trail that could be misused.
- **Outcome:** The report's findings are clear to anyone who reads it. No one has read it.

### Not Escalating to Corporate IT
- **Situation:** After Michael refused to engage with the report, Toby considered sending it directly to corporate IT to trigger a remediation.
- **What he considered:** Bypassing Michael would be procedurally appropriate (the vulnerability is a corporate-level concern) but would further damage Toby's working relationship with the branch manager — a relationship he depends on for daily HR operations.
- **What he chose:** He has not escalated yet. He is still weighing the decision.
- **Reasoning:** Toby's tradeoff calculus: fixing the vulnerability (important but not urgent, since the planted content is causing organizational confusion but not material harm) vs. the certain deterioration of his position at the branch (Michael would view the bypass as betrayal). The math hasn't tipped yet. It might.
- **Outcome:** Pending.

### Documenting Creed's Anomalous Tags
- **Situation:** Toby discovered that Creed's profile contains tags that don't exist in the system's standard taxonomy. He attempted to investigate.
- **What he considered:** Escalating to corporate, continuing to pursue meetings with Creed, or documenting and filing.
- **What he chose:** Document and file. After three failed meeting attempts (each weirder than the last), Toby added the documentation to Creed's HR file and decided that some investigations end not with resolution but with the investigator running out of energy.
- **Reasoning:** The anomalous tags are not causing obvious harm. Every interaction with Creed about the topic makes the situation less clear. The cost of continued investigation exceeds the likely benefit, and Toby has higher-priority items (the security vulnerability) that are also being ignored but are at least addressable in theory.
- **Outcome:** Creed's HR file now contains the most thorough documentation of the anomalous tags. The tags remain unexplained. Creed continues to claim they've already discussed it.

### Participating in Dwight's "Coordination Meetings"
- **Situation:** Dwight began scheduling meetings with Toby to "coordinate" their parallel investigations, which Toby quickly identified as monitoring.
- **What he considered:** Refusing the meetings (which would raise Dwight's suspicion), sharing his full findings (which would tip off the likely perpetrator), or participating strategically.
- **What he chose:** Strategic participation. Toby attends the meetings, shares general progress indicators, and withholds the specific findings that point to Dwight.
- **Reasoning:** Maintaining access to information about what Dwight is doing (or not doing) in his own investigation is more valuable than confronting Dwight prematurely. Toby is playing a longer game than Dwight realizes.
- **Outcome:** The coordination meetings continue. Dwight believes Toby's investigation is making slow progress. Toby believes Dwight is monitoring him. Both are correct.
