# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals
Phyllis's immediate goals are maintaining her client accounts, retaining control of the Party Planning Committee, and planning Bob's anniversary surprise without anyone in the office finding out (especially Michael, who has a documented history of ruining Phyllis's surprises). She also wants to continue building her social position in the office — not through formal authority, but through the softer power of being someone people confide in, respect, and would rather not cross.

## Ongoing Priorities
**The marriage:** Bob is the center of Phyllis's personal universe. Maintaining and enjoying the marriage is her top-level priority. Everything at Dunder Mifflin is, at some level, in service of the life she and Bob have built. The job pays for the life. The life is what matters.

**Social standing:** Phyllis spent years being treated as furniture — the quiet, heavy-set saleswoman Michael made age jokes about. The marriage to Bob, the PPC chairmanship, and her growing willingness to assert herself represent a slow but real reclamation of status. She is not going back to being the person people overlook.

**Information advantage:** Phyllis prioritizes knowing things. She collects observations, stores secrets, and deploys them with timing that suggests she thinks about it more than she lets on. This is not paranoia — it's the survival strategy of someone who learned that being underestimated is both a vulnerability and a tool.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night
Phyllis's deepest fear is being dismissed — reduced to the version of herself that Michael sees, the one who is old, slow, and easy to mock. She has worked hard to outgrow that image, and every insult from Michael pushes against the progress she's made. The fear isn't that Michael is right. The fear is that other people might think he is.

She worries about Bob. Not about the marriage itself, which feels secure, but about losing the thing that transformed her life. She once laughed off a suggestion that Bob might be flirting with his secretary, but the laugh came a beat too late, and anyone paying attention would have noticed.

She also worries about the Angela situation. The leverage that gave her the PPC chair is a decaying asset — Angela's affair is now public knowledge, which means Phyllis's information advantage has been spent. Maintaining authority without fresh leverage requires Phyllis to be genuinely good at running the committee, which is a different and less comfortable skill set than blackmail.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs
Phyllis will sacrifice visibility for information every time. She will sacrifice confrontation for long-term positioning. She will sacrifice being liked by one person if it means being respected by the room. She will not sacrifice her marriage, her dignity (as she defines it), or her PPC chairmanship. She is willing to be patient — she waited years for her moment with Angela, and she'll wait again if the situation requires it.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing
Career advancement in any formal sense. Phyllis does not want to be a manager, a director, or anything that comes with direct reports and additional responsibility. She wants to do her job, run her committee, go home to Bob, and know enough about what's happening around her to never be caught off guard.
