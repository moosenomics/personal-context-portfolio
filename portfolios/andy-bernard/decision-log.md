# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach
Andy makes decisions emotionally first and rationalizes them second. His gut response is almost always driven by which option makes him feel most included, most impressive, or most liked. He then constructs a logic framework after the fact, often involving an analogy that doesn't quite track. He is capable of strategic thinking — his Stamford-to-Scranton transition strategy was genuinely calculated — but strategy tends to collapse when his emotional needs override it.

## What They Want Before Deciding
Andy wants reassurance that his instinct is correct. He will poll multiple people, not to gather diverse perspectives, but to find someone who agrees with him. If the first person disagrees, he'll keep asking until he gets the answer he wants. He also wants to know what Michael would do, not because Michael's judgment is sound, but because alignment with Michael feels safe.

## How They Handle Uncertainty
Poorly. Uncertainty triggers Andy's insecurity cascade, which manifests as either manic over-activity (making decisions faster to outrun the doubt) or paralysis dressed up as "taking my time to get this right." When truly stuck, he sings. If the singing doesn't resolve the anxiety, he calls his mother, which usually makes things worse.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Proposing to Angela
- **Situation:** Andy had been dating Angela for approximately seven months. The relationship was stable in the sense that Angela hadn't ended it, which Andy interpreted as deep compatibility. He had been carrying an engagement ring in his wallet for six years ("you never know when you'll meet the right girl").
- **What he considered:** Timing (Toby's farewell party, during Jim's fireworks — maximum audience), Angela's recent warmth (she had kissed him on the lips, which was above her baseline), and his own readiness (six years of wallet ring).
- **What he chose:** Proposed publicly, in front of the entire office, at maximum romantic spectacle.
- **Reasoning:** Andy believed the grand gesture would move Angela emotionally and that a public proposal would produce the kind of memory they'd tell their children about. He also, less consciously, wanted witnesses — a public "yes" is harder to walk back.
- **Outcome:** Angela said "Okay." Andy chose to hear this as "Yes." The office applauded politely. Andy has not revisited whether "Okay" and "Yes" mean the same thing.

### Selecting Schrute Farms as Wedding Venue
- **Situation:** Angela had rejected every venue Andy proposed — EPCOT, Marriott, hot-air balloons. Andy was running out of ideas and non-refundable deposits. Angela presented a list of requirements that, coincidentally, only one location in the region could satisfy.
- **What he considered:** Angela's happiness (paramount), budget (damaged by previous deposits), venue aesthetics (Andy wanted waterfront; Angela wanted rustic).
- **What he chose:** Agreed to Schrute Farms, Dwight's beet farm, without questioning why his fiancée's dream wedding venue happened to be his workplace rival's property.
- **Reasoning:** "Angela's happiness is my happiness." Also, Andy reasoned that choosing Schrute Farms would demonstrate his flexibility and show Angela he's willing to compromise.
- **Outcome:** The venue tours have been awkward. Dwight is suspiciously enthusiastic about the booking. Andy attributes this to Dwight's entrepreneurial spirit rather than any personal motivation.

### How Andy Responded to the Profile System Rollout
- **Situation:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton rolled out personal context portfolios served via MCP. Every employee was asked to fill out their profiles.
- **What he considered:** Whether to treat the profile as a work document or a personal brand opportunity.
- **What he chose:** Andy treated it as a Cornell admissions essay. He wrote and rewrote his profile three times, optimizing for impressiveness. He included extensive sections on Here Comes Treble, his stint at Bear Stearns (brief, pre-collapse), and his "leadership philosophy" (a collection of platitudes organized by sailing metaphor). He added himself to the Finer Things Club before being asked to remove that claim.
- **Reasoning:** Andy saw the profile as the first system at Dunder Mifflin that would give everyone's AI a permanent impression of who he is. This, to Andy, was the most important first impression he'd ever make — more important than a job interview, because the AI would reference it forever.
- **Outcome:** Andy's profile is significantly longer than anyone else's in the office. His AI assistant has been generating emails that sound like admissions essays. He has asked Pam twice if profile view counts are available. When he learned that Michael and Dwight were reading people's profiles for tactical purposes, Andy's response was not to hide content but to make his profile even more impressive, reasoning that "if Michael's reading mine, this is my shot."

### Choosing What to Redact
- **Situation:** After watching Michael ambush people with profile-sourced personal details and Dwight weaponize profile information in conversations, Andy had to decide what to hide.
- **What he considered:** Andy initially didn't want to hide anything — his instinct was that a more visible profile meant more social capital. But after Michael referenced Andy's "leadership philosophy" in a meeting with the tone of someone reading a child's diary aloud, Andy reconsidered.
- **What he chose:** Andy added exclude tags for michael-scott and dwight-schrute on his most personally revealing sections — his honest fears, his Profile Optimization Project (because it's embarrassing to be caught optimizing), and the Five Families alliance (because Michael would either co-opt it or mock it). He left all his impressive-sounding content public, reasoning that there's no downside to Michael's AI telling Michael that Andy went to Cornell.
- **Reasoning:** "Hide the soft stuff, keep the strong stuff visible."
- **Outcome:** Andy's public profile reads like a LinkedIn page for someone who peaked in college. His redacted sections read like a therapy intake form. The gap between the two is the most honest thing about Andy's profile.
