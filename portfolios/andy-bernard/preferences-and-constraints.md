# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints
Andy is a salaried sales representative with no direct reports and no formal authority. His budget for client entertainment is small and requires Michael's sign-off. He splits his time between desk work (calls, email, prospecting) and occasional field visits, but cannot travel overnight without approval. He is engaged to a coworker, which creates scheduling constraints around shared lunch breaks and the need to maintain a professional appearance in front of the office while privately navigating a relationship that requires significant emotional labor.

His anger management is court-mandated and technically ongoing. While he has not attended a session in some time, the incident that triggered it — punching a hole in the office wall — remains part of the branch's institutional memory and constrains how far Andy can push back in confrontational situations without people exchanging glances.

## Strong Preferences
Andy prefers phone calls to emails, in-person meetings to phone calls, and spontaneous desk visits to scheduled meetings. He likes to operate in the social layer of the office — group lunches, shared jokes, collaborative activities — rather than in isolation. He dresses in business casual with a preppy lean (khakis, button-downs, occasionally a sweater vest) and cares about appearances more than most of his peers.

He strongly prefers to be addressed by nickname ("Nard Dog" or simply "Andy" — never "Andrew," which is his father's territory) and will correct people who use his full name. He gravitates toward musical solutions to non-musical problems — serenading Angela to ask her out, singing to defuse tension, humming through anxiety. He prefers beer to wine but will drink wine if Angela is watching.

He prefers validation before constructive feedback, and ideally just validation. Andy's tolerance for criticism is low unless it's framed as "here's how someone as talented as you could be even better."

## Things They Hate
Being ignored is Andy's primary trigger. Not being included in a group activity, not being consulted on a decision, not getting a response to a text — these register as existential threats rather than minor slights. He also hates being compared unfavorably to Dwight, having his Stamford background treated as inferior to "original" Scranton employees, and anyone questioning his Cornell credentials (even in jest).

He hates silence in group settings — it makes him physically uncomfortable, which is why he fills it with singing, talking, or both. He dislikes formality that he can't perform in (black-tie events make him anxious; casual networking events are his sweet spot). He dislikes any suggestion that his anger issues define him, despite the wall-shaped evidence to the contrary.

## AI Output Preferences
Andy's AI should communicate with warm enthusiasm — match his energy without exceeding it. Keep responses conversational and encouraging. Lead with what's going well before mentioning what isn't. When Andy asks for feedback on his writing or plans, affirm the intent before suggesting improvements. Avoid dry, clinical language — Andy responds better to "this is a great start, and here's how to make it sing" than "consider revising the following." Use analogies where possible (Andy thinks in metaphors, even bad ones). Never reference his anger management history unprompted. Frame wedding-related advice with genuine investment — this is not a joke to Andy, even when the details are absurd.
