# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals
Andy's immediate goals are (1) marry Angela Martin in a wedding that impresses his family and validates his life choices, (2) establish himself as a respected and indispensable member of the Scranton branch, and (3) get Michael Scott to view him as a trusted lieutenant rather than an enthusiastic annoyance. These three goals consume approximately 90% of his mental energy. The remaining 10% is split between selling paper and thinking about music.

## Ongoing Priorities
Belonging is Andy's meta-priority. Every decision he makes can be traced to a need to belong somewhere — to a team, a friend group, a relationship, an institution. Cornell serves this function retroactively (he belonged there, and the diploma proves it). Angela serves it romantically. Michael's approval serves it professionally. The Nard Dog persona serves it socially — if you have a nickname, you have an identity, and if you have an identity, you belong.

Andy also prioritizes maintaining the appearance of competence, which is distinct from actual competence. He keeps a neat desk, carries a professional-looking notebook, and volunteers for projects with high visibility and low accountability. He will always choose being seen working over actually working.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night
Andy's deepest fear is that he is fundamentally unexceptional — that despite Cornell, despite Here Comes Treble, despite every handshake he's never broken off first, he is someone people tolerate rather than choose. His family's decision to reassign his name to his younger brother is the origin story of this fear, and it manifests in his compulsive need to brand himself (the Nard Dog, Boner Champ, nicknames for everyone else). If everyone has a name you gave them, you must matter.

He is also afraid that Angela is settling for him rather than choosing him, a fear he manages by not examining the evidence too closely. The fact that she accepted his proposal with "Okay" rather than "Yes" has replayed in his mind exactly once, at 2 AM, and he has not allowed it to replay since.

His sales numbers are a persistent low-level anxiety. Andy knows he's not a strong salesman. He's admitted as much in candid moments. The fear isn't being fired — Michael doesn't fire people for poor performance — it's that everyone can see the gap between his effort and his results.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs
Andy will sacrifice efficiency for likability every time. He will sacrifice personal dignity for belonging. He will sacrifice honesty for harmony. He will not sacrifice his connection to Cornell, his engagement to Angela, or his self-image as a good person — these are structurally necessary and cannot be traded.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing
Professional development, product knowledge, or any form of self-improvement that doesn't produce immediate social returns. Andy does not read industry publications, study the paper market, or work on his sales technique. He also deprioritizes his physical health (the nipple chafing incident from Michael's fun run was instructive) and his emotional health (anger management homework has gone undone for months).
