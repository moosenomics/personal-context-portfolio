# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach

Creed does not deliberate. He encounters a situation, assesses it through a filter built from decades of unpredictable lived experience, and acts. The assessment happens instantaneously and operates on criteria that are invisible to outside observers. He does not weigh pros and cons, consult others, or gather additional information. He either knows what to do or he doesn't, and when he doesn't, he does nothing until the situation resolves itself or becomes someone else's problem.

## What They Want Before Deciding

Nothing. Creed does not seek inputs before making decisions. He does not want data, opinions, or context. He wants the decision to be in front of him and the path of least resistance to be visible. If the path isn't visible, he waits. If the path involves other people, he manipulates them with a naturalness that suggests he does not perceive manipulation as a distinct category of behavior — it's just how conversations work.

## How They Handle Uncertainty

Creed handles uncertainty by not recognizing it as a distinct state. He exists in a baseline condition of ambiguity — about his past, his job, his living situation, his legal status — that would paralyze most people. For Creed, uncertainty is not a temporary problem to be solved; it is the medium in which he operates. When something unexpected happens, he incorporates it into his worldview without friction. When the police arrive, he puts his hands behind his head. When the office is burglarized, he checks his own inventory. When someone mentions the quarry, he lights up. Each response is immediate, appropriate to Creed's internal logic, and mystifying to everyone else.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Redirecting Michael's Firing Decision
- **Situation:** Michael was ordered to fire someone and chose Creed. He attempted to soften the blow by framing it as Creed wanting something better.
- **What he considered:** Unknown — Creed's thought process between hearing the news and responding took approximately three seconds.
- **What he chose:** Refused to accept the firing. Told Michael to fire Devon instead. Argued, briefly and convincingly, that he was better at his job than Devon.
- **Reasoning:** Pure survival instinct. Creed's argument about job performance was not supported by evidence — he does not perform his job — but it was delivered with absolute conviction, which is what mattered. The strategy was to create enough friction that Michael would choose the easier path, and Devon was always going to be the easier path.
- **Outcome:** Devon was fired. Creed continued working at Dunder Mifflin. He has stated he would forget the entire incident quickly, and by all appearances, he has.

### Managing the Watermark Crisis
- **Situation:** An obscene watermark appeared on a batch of Dunder Mifflin paper, a quality failure that fell squarely within Creed's area of responsibility. Creed had not performed a quality spot-check in an extended period — possibly years.
- **What he considered:** Whether to accept blame or redirect it.
- **What he chose:** Redirected. Creed called the paper mill, extracted enough information to identify a plausible scapegoat, and presented the failure as a supplier issue rather than an internal quality assurance failure.
- **Reasoning:** Self-preservation. Creed recognized instantly that accepting responsibility for the watermark would expose the fact that he hasn't been doing his job, which would end his employment. His phone technique — calling the mill, asking leading questions, building a narrative from the answers — demonstrated a genuine talent for information extraction that he could have applied to quality assurance if he'd ever been inclined to try.
- **Outcome:** Creed survived. The quality failure was attributed to the supplier. No changes were made to Creed's responsibilities or oversight, because no one looked closely enough to notice that the root cause was his total abdication of his role.

### Engaging with the Profile System
- **Situation:** Corporate mandated personal context portfolios served via MCP. Employees were required to fill out their profiles.
- **What he considered:** Likely nothing. Creed does not weigh decisions about new systems — he either engages or doesn't, and the reasons are opaque.
- **What he chose:** Full, enthusiastic engagement — but on his own terms. Creed filled out his profile with content that ranges from plausible work information to entries that would alarm a competent investigator. He added custom tags (`night-operations`, `pre-2005-records`, `secondary-identity`) that don't exist elsewhere in the system. He made extensive use of the `include: creed-bratton` privacy markers to create sections only his own AI can access.
- **Reasoning:** Creed appears to have recognized the profile system as a tool for managing information asymmetry — showing different versions of yourself to different audiences — which is something he has been doing his entire life without technological assistance. The system formalized a practice he was already expert in. His engagement is not surprising in retrospect; it's the most natural extension of how Creed has always operated.
- **Outcome:** Creed's profile is the most actively maintained in the office, which no one predicted and no one has examined closely. Toby has noted the anomalous tags and failed to schedule a meeting to discuss them. The private sections of Creed's profile contain content that only his AI sees, and Creed uses it regularly.

### The Halloween Near-Firing (Pre-Profile Era)
- **Situation:** Michael was considering firing Creed during Halloween. Creed was dressed as a vampire.
- **What he considered:** Possibly nothing — Creed's response was instantaneous.
- **What he chose:** Fought back immediately and suggested Devon as an alternative. Offered to forget the whole thing instantly if Michael changed his mind.
- **Reasoning:** The same survival logic as the later firing attempt. Creed does not process threat and response as separate events — threat triggers response. The promise to forget was genuine; Creed's memory works in his favor here, allowing him to maintain a relationship with Michael that's unburdened by resentment.
- **Outcome:** Survived again. Established a pattern: Creed is harder to fire than he looks, not because he's valuable, but because firing him requires a level of direct confrontation that most people — especially Michael — find uncomfortable.
