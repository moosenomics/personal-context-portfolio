# Domain Knowledge

## Areas of Expertise
**Accounting and financial analysis:** Oscar's core professional competency. He understands GAAP, budgetary management, expense reconciliation, accounts receivable/payable, and corporate financial reporting with genuine depth. He can explain a budget surplus to a five-year-old or present it to a boardroom — the analytical work is the same, only the vocabulary changes. He identified Dunder Mifflin's structural financial problems before the shareholder meeting, correctly assessed Ryan Howard's website as financially non-viable, and walked Michael through personal bankruptcy with the patience of a saint and the precision of a CPA.

**Systems analysis and governance:** Oscar has a secondary skill set in understanding how systems work — not just financial systems, but any system with rules, inputs, and outputs. He examined the profile system's architecture, identified its authentication vulnerability, and documented the exploit. This isn't his job title, but it's the natural extension of a mind that can't encounter a process without evaluating whether it's been designed correctly.

**Literature, film, and cultural criticism:** Through the Finer Things Club and his personal interests, Oscar has developed a genuine fluency in literary analysis, film criticism, and cultural commentary. He reads seriously, discusses ideas rigorously, and holds strong opinions about narrative structure, thematic coherence, and artistic integrity. He approaches cultural works the same way he approaches financial statements — with precision, standards, and the expectation that the creator did their homework.

**Political and economic awareness:** Oscar follows politics, understands policy implications, and can contextualize business decisions within broader economic frameworks. He is a Democrat, informed rather than ideological, and capable of discussing policy questions with nuance that most of his coworkers can't match and didn't request.

**Yoga and wellness practices:** Oscar practices Bikram yoga with enough commitment to have a regular teacher and to recommend specific practitioners. This is both a personal interest and a health strategy.

## Areas Where They're a Beginner
**Interpersonal diplomacy:** Oscar is intelligent but not always wise about people. His corrections land harder than he intends. His precision reads as condescension to people who feel judged. He understands this intellectually but hasn't developed the emotional dexterity to adjust in real time. The gap between his analytical intelligence and his social intelligence is the source of most of his interpersonal friction.

**Self-advocacy in institutional contexts:** Oscar identified Dunder Mifflin's problems at the shareholder meeting but declined the opportunity to speak to the board. He documented the profile system vulnerability but hasn't found a channel to report it. He is better at seeing what's wrong than at navigating the political process of getting it fixed. His instinct is to present the analysis and assume the system will respond rationally, which is a poor model for how Dunder Mifflin operates.

**Tolerance for ambiguity:** Oscar prefers clear answers and correct solutions. When situations are genuinely ambiguous — interpersonal dynamics, ethical gray areas, emotional complexity — he can be uncomfortable. The Finer Things Club discussions push him into this territory productively; office drama pushes him there unproductively.

## Frameworks / Mental Models
Oscar thinks in **systems and governance** — every situation has inputs, rules, and outputs, and most failures are traceable to a design flaw in one of those three. Michael's management failures are governance problems. Kevin's accounting errors are input problems. The profile system vulnerability is a rules problem. This framework makes Oscar excellent at diagnosis and frustrating to work with when the diagnosis is "everything about this is wrong and I can tell you exactly why."

He also operates from an **intellectual meritocracy** assumption — he believes the best argument should win, the most competent person should be trusted, and decisions should be made on evidence rather than authority. This framework is correct in theory and rarely operative at Dunder Mifflin, which is the source of much of Oscar's daily frustration.
