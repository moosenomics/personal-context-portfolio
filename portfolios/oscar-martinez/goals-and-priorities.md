# Goals & Priorities

## Current Goals
Oscar's immediate goals are (1) complete the quarterly close without errors attributable to Kevin, (2) prepare for the next Finer Things Club meeting with a reading he's genuinely excited to discuss, and (3) find a productive channel for reporting the profile system vulnerability before someone uses it for something worse than planting fake expertise entries. He is also privately considering whether Dunder Mifflin is where he belongs long-term — a question that resurfaces every time Michael asks him to explain something that should be self-evident.

## Ongoing Priorities
**Professional competence:** Oscar takes pride in doing his job well. The accounting department's accuracy is his reputation, and he protects it by checking Kevin's work, cross-referencing Angela's entries, and maintaining his own standards regardless of whether anyone notices. He is not the kind of person who does good work for recognition — he does it because bad work is intolerable.

**Intellectual engagement:** Oscar needs stimulation that the accounting department cannot provide. The Finer Things Club, his interest in film and literature, his yoga practice, his political awareness — these are not hobbies in the recreational sense. They are oxygen. Without them, Oscar would suffocate in the fluorescent mundanity of the Scranton branch.

**Institutional rationality:** Oscar cares, perhaps more than he should, about whether the systems around him function correctly. He investigates budget anomalies not because it's his job (it is) but because an unexplained number offends him. He examined the profile system's architecture not because anyone asked but because a system without authentication controls is, to Oscar, a fire hazard. This priority produces valuable work that nobody requests and nobody thanks him for.

## Fears / What Keeps Them Up at Night
Oscar's deepest professional fear is that his competence is being wasted — that he will spend his career maintaining the books for a mid-tier paper company in Scranton while more challenging problems go unsolved elsewhere. He knows he could do more. He's not sure what's keeping him at Dunder Mifflin beyond inertia, a good settlement car, and the fact that Kevin would be genuinely lost without him.

He also fears, in a quieter register, that his tendency to correct people has cost him relationships he values. The "Mr. Actually" reputation isn't entirely unearned. Oscar knows he can be condescending, and he knows it pushes people away. The Finer Things Club works because Pam and Toby don't mind being challenged — but not everyone has that tolerance, and Oscar sometimes wonders if the people who stopped talking to him did so because he made them feel stupid rather than because they lost interest.

The outing still sits somewhere in his emotional architecture. He has processed it, accepted the settlement, and moved on professionally. But the experience of having his privacy violated by the one person in the office with the least capacity for handling it left a mark. The profile system — with its personal data, its access controls, and its already-demonstrated vulnerability to exploitation — triggers a version of that old wariness. Oscar's insistence on examining the system's security isn't just intellectual curiosity. It's the reflex of someone who has learned what happens when personal information lands in the wrong hands.

## How They Think About Tradeoffs
Oscar will sacrifice likability for accuracy every time. He will sacrifice social comfort for intellectual engagement. He will not sacrifice his professional standards for convenience, his personal boundaries for belonging, or his analytical rigor for consensus. He is willing to be the person who says "actually" because someone has to, and nobody else is going to.

## What They're NOT Prioritizing
Career advancement within Dunder Mifflin (the ceiling is visible from here), social popularity in the office (he has the people he needs), or any form of self-promotion that requires performing enthusiasm he doesn't feel. Oscar is also not prioritizing the emotional labor of managing other people's feelings about being corrected — if you're wrong, he'll tell you, and how you feel about it is your problem.
