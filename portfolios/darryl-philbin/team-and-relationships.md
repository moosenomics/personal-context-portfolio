# Team & Relationships

## Michael Scott
- **Role:** Regional Manager
- **Relationship:** Boss in title, recurring source of workplace hazards and casual racial insensitivity in practice
- **Interaction pattern:** Darryl manages Michael the way you'd manage a well-meaning but poorly trained dog — with patience, firm boundaries, and the occasional treat to reinforce good behavior. Michael calls Darryl "Mittah Rogers" (a nickname that evolved through several iterations), disrupts the warehouse with alarming regularity, and has once directly caused Darryl to break his ankle. Despite this, Darryl maintains a functional working relationship by picking his battles — he's firm about safety, flexible about everything else, and has learned that Michael responds better to directness than to subtlety.
- **What Darryl needs from Michael:** To stay out of the warehouse, to approve capital requests without a three-week conversation, and to stop assuming things about Darryl based on stereotypes he doesn't realize he holds.
- **What Michael needs from Darryl:** Validation that he's cool. Michael wants Darryl to like him in a way that goes beyond professional respect, and Darryl knows this. He occasionally provides it — participating in Threat Level Midnight, humoring the "fluffy fingers" conversation — because the cost is low and the goodwill it buys is high.
- **Context:** Darryl has taught Michael several fake cultural concepts — handshakes, slang, "street" knowledge — that Michael believes are genuine. Darryl does this partly for his own amusement and partly because it's easier than correcting Michael's assumptions directly. The dynamic is unequal in a way that Darryl is fully aware of and Michael is not.

## Warehouse Crew
- **Role:** Dock workers, forklift operators, shipping staff
- **Relationship:** Darryl's team — the people he is actually responsible for and who actually respect his authority
- **Interaction pattern:** Darryl leads with casual competence. He doesn't micromanage, doesn't yell, and doesn't play favorites. He assigns work clearly, handles problems himself when he can, and goes to bat for his crew when the office upstairs creates issues that affect their work. The crew reciprocates with reliability and loyalty.
- **What Darryl needs from the crew:** Show up, work safe, get shipments out. Everything else is negotiable.
- **What the crew needs from Darryl:** A buffer between them and the office. Someone who understands warehouse work because he's done it himself.
- **Context:** The warehouse is its own ecosystem within Dunder Mifflin — different culture, different pace, different priorities. Darryl is the bridge between that world and the office upstairs, and he takes the role seriously.

## Jim Halpert
- **Role:** Sales Representative
- **Relationship:** The office contact Darryl works with most frequently and the one he respects the most
- **Interaction pattern:** Jim and Darryl interact primarily around fulfillment coordination — rush orders, client-specific packaging, shipping timelines. Jim is reasonable about what he asks for and receptive when Darryl pushes back on unrealistic deadlines, which makes him Darryl's preferred sales contact. Outside of work, they have an easy rapport — they watch games together, joke about the office dysfunction, and share a perspective on Dunder Mifflin that's more clear-eyed than most.
- **What Darryl needs from Jim:** Realistic lead times on orders and advance notice on spikes.
- **What Jim needs from Darryl:** Reliable execution on fulfillment and someone to confirm that the things happening upstairs are as absurd as they look from the sales floor.

## Andy Bernard
- **Role:** Sales Representative
- **Relationship:** Collegial but uneven — Andy's need for approval and his tendency toward intensity occasionally test Darryl's patience
- **Interaction pattern:** Andy and Darryl's relationship is friendly but asymmetric. Andy seeks Darryl's validation more actively than Darryl seeks anything from Andy. Darryl tolerates this with good humor most of the time and genuine irritation when Andy's enthusiasm overrides his awareness of social cues.
- **Context:** Darryl once paid Creed three dollars to make a threatening gesture at Andy as part of a joke. This says something about both the economy of favors in the office and Darryl's willingness to use available resources for entertainment.

## Kelly Kapoor
- **Role:** Customer Service Representative
- **Relationship:** Ex-girlfriend — a brief, rebound-driven relationship that ended because Darryl saw the pattern clearly and chose not to participate in it
- **Context:** Darryl dated Kelly after his divorce, initially as a rebound that he was honest about. Kelly described Darryl as "the most complicated man I have ever met" because he says exactly what he's thinking — a communication style she found baffling because directness was not what she was used to from Ryan. Darryl told Kelly she needed to "access her uncrazy side," which was the most direct relationship feedback anyone in the office has ever delivered. The relationship ended. Darryl does not dwell on it.

## Dwight Schrute
- **Role:** Assistant (to the) Regional Manager / Senior Sales Representative
- **Relationship:** Minimal. Dwight occasionally attempts to assert authority over the warehouse, and Darryl deflects this with the energy of someone swatting a fly.
- **Context:** Dwight once investigated Darryl's workers' compensation claim and was stymied. Darryl finds Dwight more annoying than threatening and gives him exactly as much attention as required to make him go away.
