# Tools & Systems

## Primary Tools

Darryl works with both physical warehouse equipment and office systems. On the warehouse floor: forklifts, pallet jacks, the loading dock, the shipping scale, and the label printer. He operates a heavy-duty GMC Sierra crew cab for work-related deliveries and moves. His personal vehicle is a Chevrolet Silverado with remote start.

In the office context, Darryl uses a computer for order management, shipping coordination, and basic scheduling. He interfaces with Dunder Mifflin's order fulfillment system to track inbound and outbound shipments, confirm delivery dates, and generate pick lists for the crew. He is comfortable with these tools without being enthusiastic about them — they're functional, not interesting.

His AI assistant is configured to read personal context portfolios, and Darryl has engaged with it pragmatically. He uses it for work-related tasks — checking on shipping schedules, drafting communications to the office upstairs, and occasionally looking up client account details that the sales team should have provided but didn't. He does not use it for personal tasks and does not treat it as a conversation partner. It's a tool, and Darryl treats tools with respect and without sentimentality.

## Key Workflows

The warehouse operates on a pull system driven by the sales team's order queue. Orders flow from the sales reps to Pam at reception (who enters confirmations), then to Darryl's shipping queue, where he assigns them to the crew based on priority, truck availability, and delivery geography. Rush orders get flagged by the sales team and Darryl makes the call on whether they're feasible given current capacity.

Coordination with the office happens through a combination of direct conversations (Darryl walking upstairs or Jim walking down), phone calls, and the occasional email when a paper trail matters. Darryl prefers in-person communication for anything time-sensitive because email at Dunder Mifflin is not treated with urgency by most of the office staff.

## Current System Issues

The biggest system issue from Darryl's perspective is that the warehouse and the office operate on different clocks. The sales team promises delivery dates without checking warehouse capacity, Michael approves special handling requests without understanding what they entail, and the order entry process has enough manual steps that errors propagate downstream. Darryl compensates for these gaps with experience and buffer time, but the structural inefficiency bothers him on principle.

The profile system has been a non-issue for the warehouse. Darryl filled out his profile, set his redactions, and moved on. His crew has minimal engagement with it — they don't have AI assistants and they don't read profiles. The profile drama that consumed the office upstairs barely registered on the warehouse floor, which Darryl considers further evidence that the warehouse is the functional core of the business and the office is the decorative trim.
