# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints

Darryl's schedule is structured around two immovable commitments: the warehouse shipping schedule (weekdays) and his custody arrangement with Jada (alternating weekends and designated evenings). Everything else flexes around these. He will not work weekends when he has Jada, he will not stay late when he has an evening handoff, and he will not accept assignments that compromise the warehouse's ability to ship on time.

He works in the warehouse, physically separated from the office upstairs. This creates a structural constraint on his visibility to management and corporate — he is literally out of sight for most of the decision-making that happens at Dunder Mifflin, which limits his ability to advocate for himself in real time.

## Strong Preferences

Darryl prefers efficiency over ceremony. Meetings that could be emails annoy him. Processes that exist for bureaucratic rather than functional reasons frustrate him. He'd rather fix a problem himself in five minutes than spend thirty minutes talking about it in the conference room.

He dresses practically for warehouse work — button-up shirts or polos, work boots when he's on the floor, slightly more polished when he knows he'll be upstairs for an extended period. His appearance is deliberate without being fussy. He looks like someone who takes his job seriously and himself lightly.

Darryl enjoys basketball, music (playing and listening), spending time with Jada, feeding squirrels, and social events where the vibe is relaxed and the expectations are low. He does not enjoy forced fun, corporate team-building, or any activity where Michael is the emcee.

He prefers to eat lunch in the warehouse break area rather than the office break room, partly because the food politics upstairs are exhausting and partly because the warehouse has a better atmosphere during the midday lull.

## Things They Hate

**Safety violations.** Darryl takes warehouse safety seriously because the consequences of getting it wrong are immediate and physical — not theoretical, not political, physical. Michael's history of disrupting the warehouse with zero regard for safety protocols is Darryl's single biggest source of professional frustration. The ankle incident crystallized this: Michael treated the warehouse like a playground and Darryl paid the price.

**Being underestimated.** Darryl does not broadcast his intelligence or ambition, which means people occasionally mistake his mellow demeanor for a lack of either. This is a miscalculation he corrects quietly, through results rather than arguments, but it still bothers him when it happens.

**Indirect communication.** Darryl does not have patience for people who won't say what they mean. Kelly's relationship style — saying one thing, meaning another, expecting Darryl to decode the difference — was fundamentally incompatible with how he operates. He'd rather have an uncomfortable direct conversation than a comfortable indirect one.

**Being treated as a stereotype.** Michael's assumptions about Darryl — the gang questions, the made-up cultural exchanges — are something Darryl manages with humor and strategic deflection. But the underlying dynamic — a white boss who doesn't realize he's being racist — is one Darryl navigates daily, and the emotional cost of that navigation is higher than he lets on.

**The glass ceiling he can see from the warehouse floor.** Darryl is aware that his path to advancement at Dunder Mifflin is structurally limited. The warehouse manager role doesn't naturally pipeline into office management or corporate positions. The people making hiring and promotion decisions don't spend time on the warehouse floor. And the one person who could advocate for Darryl to corporate — Michael — doesn't fully understand what Darryl contributes. This is the constraint Darryl thinks about most and talks about least.

## AI Output Preferences

Keep it practical and actionable. Darryl doesn't want analysis for its own sake — he wants information he can use to make a decision or solve a problem. Lead with the answer, follow with supporting detail if needed, and skip the preamble entirely.

Match his tone: direct, low-key, no excessive enthusiasm. Darryl doesn't want an AI that's relentlessly upbeat or that treats every task as exciting. He wants one that treats him as a competent adult who knows his job and needs help with specific, bounded tasks.

For warehouse-related queries — shipping schedules, inventory questions, crew management — respond with the specificity of someone who understands physical operations. For career-related queries, be honest about tradeoffs and constraints rather than defaulting to encouragement. Darryl can handle a realistic assessment. He prefers it.
