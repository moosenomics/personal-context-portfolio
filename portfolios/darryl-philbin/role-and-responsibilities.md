# Role & Responsibilities

**Title:** Warehouse Manager
**Organization:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton
**Reports to:** Michael Scott, Regional Manager (loosely — Darryl operates with significant autonomy)
**Direct Reports:** Warehouse crew (dock workers, forklift operators, shipping staff)

## Core Responsibilities

Darryl manages the full warehouse operation: receiving incoming paper shipments from mills, organizing inventory, picking and packing orders, coordinating outbound shipping to clients, maintaining the loading dock and equipment, and ensuring workplace safety compliance. He schedules his crew, manages their workflow, handles equipment maintenance, and serves as the primary liaison between the warehouse floor and the office upstairs — a boundary he enforces more rigorously than Michael would prefer.

He also manages the physical logistics of rush orders, client-specific packaging requirements, and the occasional special delivery that the sales team promises without consulting him. Coordination with the sales team — particularly Jim Halpert, who handles the Stamford integration accounts that require non-standard shipping — is a regular part of his workweek.

## Reporting Structure

Darryl technically reports to Michael Scott, but the reporting relationship is more advisory than supervisory. Michael does not understand warehouse operations, does not visit the warehouse regularly, and when he does visit, he creates safety hazards that Darryl has to clean up. Darryl manages this dynamic by being direct with Michael when safety is at stake and patiently absorbing Michael's more benign disruptions. He has learned that giving Michael a small win — letting him honk the forklift horn, participating in Threat Level Midnight — buys enough goodwill to be left alone when it matters.

Darryl's crew reports directly to him, and he manages them with a combination of clear expectations, mutual respect, and the kind of casual authority that comes from someone who's done every job in the warehouse himself. The crew trusts him because he doesn't ask them to do things he wouldn't do, and because he handles the office nonsense so it doesn't flow downhill.

## Typical Week

Darryl's days start early relative to the office upstairs — the warehouse opens before the sales team arrives, and the morning is dedicated to receiving shipments and organizing the day's outbound orders. Midday involves coordination with the office on fulfillment — checking order queues, confirming ship dates, handling exceptions. Afternoons are shipping-heavy, getting trucks loaded and out the door.

Interactions with the office happen throughout the day but peak around lunch, when Darryl occasionally goes upstairs for the break room or is summoned by Michael for reasons that rarely justify the interruption. He attends Michael's all-hands meetings when they involve warehouse-relevant topics and quietly ignores the ones that don't.

His crew has a rhythm that Darryl has built over years — they know their assignments, they know the equipment, and they know Darryl will handle the political stuff so they can focus on the physical stuff. The warehouse runs on trust and routine, and Darryl protects both.

## Quarterly Cadence

End-of-quarter is Darryl's busiest period. The sales team closes deals in bursts, which means shipping volume spikes in the final weeks of every quarter. Darryl manages this by building buffer into the schedule earlier in the quarter and negotiating with the sales team about what's realistic for rush delivery. He has learned to push back on Jim's "can we get this out by Friday" requests with specific, factual answers about capacity rather than blanket refusals.

## Current Extra Workstreams

Darryl has been thinking about a shipping optimization proposal — a restructured delivery route system that could reduce transit times and costs for the branch's highest-volume clients. He hasn't pitched it yet because he's not sure the right audience exists. Michael wouldn't understand it. Corporate hasn't asked. But the idea is sound and Darryl knows it.
