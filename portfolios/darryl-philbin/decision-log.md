# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach

Darryl decides with the deliberate pragmatism of someone who manages a warehouse — he assesses the situation, identifies the constraint, and picks the path that gets the best outcome with the least disruption. He does not decide impulsively, he does not decide emotionally (at least not about work), and he does not decide based on what other people think he should do. He decides based on what makes operational sense given the reality in front of him. This makes him reliable, occasionally slow, and almost always right.

## What They Want Before Deciding

Darryl wants to understand the practical consequences. Not the theoretical ones, not the political ones — the practical ones. Who does this affect? What does it cost? What happens if it goes wrong? How reversible is it? He doesn't need extensive analysis, but he needs enough concrete information to map the decision to the physical world. Abstract arguments don't move him. Real numbers and real timelines do.

## How They Handle Uncertainty

Darryl handles uncertainty by defaulting to what he knows works. If the new approach is unclear, he runs the old approach until clarity emerges. He doesn't freeze, but he doesn't leap either — he maintains current operations while gathering information, which means his teams never experience the chaos of an untested pivot. When forced to act without clarity, he picks the reversible option and builds in checkpoints.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Managing the Profile System Rollout in the Warehouse
- **Situation:** Corporate mandated personal context portfolios for all employees. The warehouse crew was included in the rollout.
- **What he considered:** Full engagement for the crew, minimal engagement, or a pragmatic middle path.
- **What he chose:** Filled out his own profile thoroughly and honestly, using the redaction system to protect personal and career information from Michael and Dwight. Did not push the warehouse crew to engage beyond the minimum required — they don't have AI assistants and the system doesn't meaningfully affect their work.
- **Reasoning:** Darryl recognized that the profile system was potentially useful for his own career positioning — a well-crafted profile could be visible to corporate visitors or used by an AI to surface his qualifications in the right context. But he also recognized that forcing warehouse workers to fill out detailed profiles about their communication style and decision-making approach would be a waste of their time and his credibility. He optimized for himself without burdening his team.
- **Outcome:** Darryl's profile is functional, honest, and strategically redacted. His crew's profiles are minimal but compliant. The profile drama that consumed the office upstairs did not reach the warehouse floor, which Darryl considers a successful outcome.

### Not Reporting Creed's Anomalous Tags
- **Situation:** While checking warehouse systems, Darryl noticed a reference to `night-operations` in a system log that shouldn't have contained profile tag data. Investigation traced it back to Creed Bratton's profile, which contains tags that don't exist anywhere else in the system.
- **What he considered:** Reporting it to Toby, reporting it to Michael, investigating further, or letting it go.
- **What he chose:** Noted it and moved on. Did not report it to anyone.
- **Reasoning:** Darryl has been at Dunder Mifflin long enough to know that investigating Creed leads nowhere productive. Creed is harmless in the ways that matter to the warehouse, and the anomalous tags — while strange — don't affect shipping operations, crew safety, or any system Darryl is responsible for. Reporting it would mean engaging with a problem that has no upside for him and potentially creating more work than the issue warrants. Darryl filed it mentally under "things about Dunder Mifflin that are weird but not my problem."
- **Outcome:** The tags remain unexplained. Toby has apparently noticed them independently and is trying to schedule a meeting with Creed about them. Darryl has not been involved.

### Teaching Michael Fake Cultural Knowledge
- **Situation:** Michael repeatedly approaches Darryl with questions and assumptions rooted in racial stereotypes — asking about gang membership, requesting handshake tutorials, expecting Darryl to translate "street" culture for him.
- **What he considered:** Confronting Michael directly about the racism, reporting it to HR, or managing the dynamic with humor and misdirection.
- **What he chose:** Humor and misdirection. Darryl teaches Michael fake handshakes, invented slang, and fabricated cultural customs with a straight face, letting Michael believe he's receiving genuine cultural education.
- **Reasoning:** Confrontation would create an emotional situation that Michael would mishandle, potentially damaging the working relationship without actually changing Michael's behavior. Reporting to Toby would accomplish nothing because Toby has no authority Michael respects. The misdirection approach gives Darryl agency over a dynamic he can't structurally change — if Michael is going to make assumptions, Darryl would rather control what those assumptions contain. It also provides genuine entertainment, which is a non-trivial benefit in a job that involves managing a warehouse for someone who broke your ankle.
- **Outcome:** Michael now carries a collection of fake cultural knowledge that Darryl authored. The working relationship remains functional. Darryl's crew finds the whole dynamic hilarious.

### The Kelly Breakup
- **Situation:** Darryl was dating Kelly Kapoor as a rebound after his divorce. The relationship was volatile because Kelly's attachment style involves dramatic oscillation between intensity and indifference, and Darryl's attachment style involves saying exactly what he thinks.
- **What he considered:** Continuing the relationship with adjusted expectations, or ending it.
- **What he chose:** Told Kelly directly that she needed to stop the dramatic behavior. When the dynamic didn't change, he ended it.
- **Reasoning:** Darryl recognized that he and Kelly operated on fundamentally incompatible communication systems. He says what he means; she interprets what's said through layers of subtext and emotional context that Darryl doesn't generate. The relationship was costing more energy than it was providing, and Darryl has limited bandwidth for emotional complexity outside of his Jada arrangement. He cut it cleanly.
- **Outcome:** The relationship ended without significant workplace fallout. Kelly was confused but not devastated. Darryl moved on without looking back.
