# Decision Log

## General Decision-Making Approach

Kevin decides based on what feels right in the moment, with limited projection of consequences and no formal framework. His decisions are fast (when the choice is clear, like food) and slow (when the choice is ambiguous, like accounting entries he doesn't understand). He does not deliberate, consult stakeholders, or weigh options against criteria — he goes with the answer that arrives first and adjusts if someone tells him it's wrong. This approach works surprisingly well in domains where intuition is a valid input (poker, cooking, social observation) and predictably poorly in domains where it isn't (accounting, personal finance, career planning).

## What They Want Before Deciding

Kevin wants simplicity. He makes better decisions when the options are clear, the consequences are immediate, and the choice doesn't require holding multiple variables in mind simultaneously. He does not want analysis, context, or additional information unless it's specifically relevant to the thing he's deciding right now. He makes decisions the way he eats — one thing at a time, with full attention on the thing in front of him.

## How They Handle Uncertainty

Kevin handles uncertainty by defaulting to the familiar. When he doesn't know what to do, he does what he did last time — enters the number the same way, plays the same song, orders the same lunch. When the familiar option isn't available, he asks Oscar (for work decisions) or goes with his gut (for everything else). Genuine uncertainty — the kind where there's no precedent and no one to ask — makes Kevin anxious in a way he manages by stalling until the situation resolves itself or someone else decides.

## Recent Significant Decisions

### Splitting from Scrantonicity to Form Scrantonicity II
- **Situation:** Creative differences within the original Scrantonicity — the band wanted to expand beyond The Police; Kevin did not.
- **What he considered:** Compromising on the setlist vs. maintaining The Police's catalog as the band's exclusive repertoire.
- **What he chose:** He split from the band and formed Scrantonicity II as a pure Police cover act.
- **Reasoning:** "If you start playing other stuff, you're just a band. If you only play The Police, you're a tribute band. Tribute bands have identity." Kevin's reasoning is musically questionable and emotionally sound — he wanted to preserve the thing he loves rather than dilute it.
- **Outcome:** Scrantonicity II exists, books gigs (including Phyllis's wedding), and gives Kevin a creative home where he makes the rules. The original Scrantonicity has not been heard from.

### Barely Filling Out His PCP Profile
- **Situation:** The office was required to fill out personal context portfolios for the new MCP system.
- **What he considered:** Filling it out thoroughly (like Angela) vs. doing the minimum (like Kevin).
- **What he chose:** Minimum. Kevin filled in basic information, made several errors, listed his title as "Accountant and Drummer," and added exclude blocks for Michael and Dwight because everyone else was doing it.
- **Reasoning:** Kevin didn't understand the system's purpose, didn't understand the redaction feature, and spent most of the rollout session asking if the AI could recommend pizza places. He did the minimum because the minimum was all he understood was being asked of him.
- **Outcome:** Kevin's profile is the thinnest in the office. His AI assistant provides restaurant recommendations, which Kevin considers a success.

### Not Telling Anyone About His Gambling Debts
- **Situation:** Kevin has accumulated gambling debts from poker games and sports betting that require management beyond his normal income.
- **What he considered:** Asking for help (from Oscar, from Michael, from family) vs. managing it himself.
- **What he chose:** Managing it himself, which has involved creative approaches to generating income (the ice cream business idea, playing more poker to win back losses) rather than reducing spending.
- **Reasoning:** Kevin associates asking for financial help with admitting a problem, and he does not classify his gambling as a problem — he classifies it as a run of bad luck that better luck will eventually correct. This is the same reasoning that keeps people gambling, and Kevin is not immune to it.
- **Outcome:** The debts are managed but not resolved. The ice cream business exists as a concept. Kevin's financial situation is stable in the way that a bridge with cracks is stable — it works until it doesn't.

### Accepting His Role in the Accounting Department
- **Situation:** Kevin applied for the warehouse and was placed in accounting by Michael. He could have pushed back, requested a transfer, or left.
- **What he chose:** He stayed. He accepted the role Michael gave him and has continued in it for years.
- **Reasoning:** Michael believed in him. Nobody had done that before — at least not for a desk job, not for something that sounded professional. Kevin stayed because being believed in felt better than being in the warehouse, even if the job itself was harder than he expected and the fit was worse than anyone would later admit.
- **Outcome:** Kevin is an accountant. He is not a great accountant. But he is a present, consistent, and good-natured member of a department that would be more efficient without him and less human.

Whether to ask out a woman named Lynn he met at the office Valentine's Day party. She seemed interested. Kevin is interested.
