# Domain Knowledge

## Areas of Expertise

**Cooking.** Kevin's culinary knowledge is genuine and deep — he has cooked his way through Julia Child's cookbook, developed his own chili recipe through years of iterative refinement, and understands ingredients, techniques, and flavor profiles at a level that would impress anyone who hasn't seen his accounting work. His chili — the Famous Kevin Malone Chili — is a legitimate culinary achievement: toasted ancho chiles, proprietary spice blends, undercooked onions (intentionally), and a preparation process he can describe with more precision than any financial procedure.

**Music and drumming.** Kevin is a skilled drummer with years of experience in cover bands. His knowledge of The Police's catalog is encyclopedic, and his technical drumming ability — timing, fills, dynamics — is solid enough to earn the band paying gigs. Music is the domain where Kevin's discipline and talent align, and his competence is unambiguous.

**Poker and probability.** Kevin earned a bracelet to compete in the World Series of Poker, which requires a level of strategic thinking, probability assessment, and pattern recognition that is completely inconsistent with his professional reputation. He reads other players well, manages risk intuitively, and understands expected value in a gambling context — a skill that has not transferred to his accounting work for reasons that are both mysterious and completely predictable.

**Basketball.** Kevin is a surprisingly excellent basketball player — a fact discovered when Michael assembled an office team and excluded Kevin. His shooting accuracy, court awareness, and athletic coordination are legitimately good, which undermines the assumption that Kevin is universally uncoordinated.

**Office social dynamics.** Kevin notices things. He was the first person to identify the Jim-Pam relationship. He picks up on emotional shifts, interpersonal tensions, and unspoken dynamics with an accuracy that surprises people who assume his observational skills match his mathematical ones. This perceptiveness is intermittent and unpredictable, but when it surfaces, it's genuinely sharp.

## Areas Where They're a Beginner

**Accounting (despite it being his job).** Kevin's technical accounting skills are marginal. He understands the basic mechanics — data entry, transaction categorization, ledger maintenance — but lacks the analytical depth, attention to detail, and error-correction instinct that the role requires. He compensates with workarounds, pattern-matching from previous entries, and Oscar's help, but the gap between his skill level and Angela's standards is the defining feature of his professional life.

**Personal finance.** Kevin's gambling debts, impulsive spending, and limited savings suggest a relationship with money management that mirrors his accounting performance — functional enough to avoid crisis (usually) but far from sound.

**Technology.** Kevin uses his computer for prescribed tasks and does not experiment, configure, or troubleshoot. When something goes wrong, he either asks someone or waits for it to resolve itself. His understanding of the PCP system is minimal — he filled out the profile because he was told to and uses the AI assistant for restaurant recommendations.

**Written communication.** Kevin's writing is functional but rough — misspellings, missing punctuation, and structures that reflect his spoken cadence (one thought per sentence, delivered without elaboration).

## Frameworks / Mental Models

Kevin's primary mental model is **immediate experience.** He evaluates situations based on how they feel right now — is this enjoyable? Is this uncomfortable? Is there food? — rather than projecting forward to consequences or backward to causes. This model produces excellent results in the kitchen (where taste is the ultimate arbiter) and poor results in accounting (where deferred consequences are the entire point).

His secondary model is **pattern recognition.** Kevin is better at spotting patterns than at understanding why they exist. He notices when something looks different from how it usually looks — whether that's a number in a spreadsheet, a facial expression in a meeting, or a change in someone's behavior. He can't always explain what the pattern means, but he flags it, and he's right more often than his reputation suggests.

His tertiary model, applied to music, is **feel.** Kevin understands rhythm, dynamics, and musical structure intuitively — he doesn't analyze a song's time signature so much as he feels when the fill should come and whether the energy should build or pull back. This is genuine musical intelligence, and it's the clearest evidence that Kevin's cognitive profile is more varied than a single dimension would suggest.
