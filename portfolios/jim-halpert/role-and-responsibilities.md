# Role & Responsibilities

**Title:** Sales Representative
**Organization:** Dunder Mifflin Scranton
**Reports to:** Michael Scott, Regional Manager
**Direct Reports:** None

## Core Responsibilities

Jim's primary function is selling paper and paper-related products to small and mid-size businesses in the Scranton region. This includes maintaining existing client accounts, pursuing new leads, preparing and delivering sales proposals, negotiating pricing, and coordinating with the warehouse on fulfillment. He also handles customer complaints, renewals, and upselling when the opportunity presents itself — though he'd never describe it that way.

He is not the top salesman at the branch (that distinction belongs to Dwight, who will remind you of this unprompted), but he is solidly in the upper tier and has been for years. Jim's approach to selling is relational rather than transactional — he builds rapport, remembers details about clients' lives, and makes the buying process painless enough that people stick around.

## Reporting Structure

Jim reports directly to Michael Scott. Functionally, this means he attends mandatory conference room meetings, participates in whatever initiative Michael has decided is urgent this week, and navigates the occasional power dynamic where Michael wants to be his friend and his boss simultaneously. Jim manages this by being agreeable enough to avoid conflict and sarcastic enough to maintain his sanity.

Dwight Schrute sits next to Jim and technically holds no authority over him, despite Dwight's repeated assertions that his role as Assistant (to the) Regional Manager grants him supervisory privileges. Jim ignores this entirely. The sales team also includes Andy Bernard, Stanley Hudson, and Phyllis Vance, with whom Jim has cordial but arms-length professional relationships.

## Typical Week

Monday through Friday follows a loose pattern: arrive slightly after 9:00, check voicemail, respond to client emails with more personality than the average paper salesman, make sales calls with visible reluctance, and fill the gaps between productive work with whatever distraction is available — usually pranking Dwight or talking to Pam at reception.

Client meetings happen two to three times per week, either on-site at the client's office or occasionally at Dunder Mifflin when Michael insists on hosting. Jim is good at these meetings because he's genuinely likable and can read a room, which compensates for his total lack of enthusiasm about the product itself.

Conference room meetings with Michael happen at unpredictable intervals, sometimes multiple times per day, and cover topics ranging from legitimate business updates to Michael's personal crises to mandatory fun activities that no one finds fun. Jim endures these with visible patience and frequent glances at the camera.

The remaining time is allocated to pranks (planning, sourcing materials, execution, savoring Dwight's reaction), conversations with Pam at the reception desk, and occasionally doing actual work when a deadline forces the issue.

## Quarterly Cadence

Quarterly sales targets drive Jim's rhythm more than he'd admit. He typically coasts through the first two months of a quarter and then closes enough deals in the final weeks to hit his numbers, a pattern that frustrates Dwight (who tracks everyone's numbers) and impresses Michael (who takes credit for it). Quarterly reviews with Michael are brief and painless because Jim's numbers speak for themselves and he's skilled at redirecting the conversation before Michael can turn it into a therapy session.

## Current Extra Workstreams

Jim is currently running a covert operation against Dwight Schrute's profile in the company's new personal context portfolio system — an operation he has internally titled "Ongoing Morale Initiative" and which Pam Beesly is fully aware of and actively assisting with.
