# Preferences & Constraints

## Hard Constraints

Jim works standard office hours at Dunder Mifflin Scranton, Monday through Friday. He has no direct reports and no formal authority over anyone in the office. His compensation is salary plus commission, which means his income varies with sales performance — a structure that works in his favor because he's consistently good without being top-of-the-board.

He sits in the bullpen directly across from Dwight Schrute, a seating arrangement he did not choose but has come to depend on for entertainment purposes. Any proposal to move his desk would be met with resistance he'd disguise as indifference.

## Strong Preferences

Jim prefers low-key interactions over formal processes. He'd rather walk over to someone's desk than send an email, rather have a conversation than attend a meeting, and rather resolve something with a joke than escalate it through channels. Bureaucracy bores him, and he'll route around it whenever possible.

He dresses in business casual that leans casual — collared shirts, no tie unless Michael mandates one for a client meeting, a general aesthetic of someone who cares enough to look presentable but not enough to look like he's trying. His hair does what it wants and he lets it.

Jim takes lunch away from his desk when he can, often with Pam or occasionally on his own when he needs to decompress from a particularly intense Michael situation. He does not eat lunch with Dwight, Andy, or Michael voluntarily, though he will accept if cornered.

He watches sports, particularly football and basketball, and can hold detailed conversations about teams and players that reveal significantly more passion than he ever shows about paper. He plays recreational sports and video games and has competitive instincts he channels into pranks rather than sales leaderboards.

## Things They Hate

**Mandatory fun.** Jim loathes any office activity where enjoyment is compulsory — Michael's improv sessions, forced team-building exercises, theme days, motivational speakers. He'll participate because the alternative is worse, but his body language radiates polite suffering.

**Being micromanaged.** Jim does his job well and expects to be left alone as a result. When Michael or Dwight attempt to exert authority over his work process, Jim responds with a level of passive resistance that's so smooth it barely registers as resistance.

**Earnestness without self-awareness.** Jim can handle earnest people — he's genuinely fond of Michael, who is nothing if not earnest. What he can't handle is earnestness combined with a complete lack of self-awareness about how one is being perceived. Andy's unironic Cornell boosterism hits this nerve. Dwight's self-appointed authority hits it harder.

**Being cornered into emotional conversations.** Jim is happy to be supportive, happy to listen, happy to offer advice with a light touch — but the moment someone tries to force him into a vulnerable conversation he hasn't chosen to have, he shuts down behind a wall of humor and deflection.

**The assumption that he's content.** Jim is aware that most of the office sees him as the well-adjusted one — the guy who's fine, who has it figured out, who doesn't need anything. This perception is useful but also grating, because it means no one ever asks whether he's actually happy or just good at seeming happy.

## AI Output Preferences

Keep it conversational and concise. Jim doesn't want formal reports, bullet-pointed analyses, or anything that feels like homework. He responds best to a tone that's direct, slightly informal, and treats him as someone who can handle complexity without needing it dressed up.

Don't over-explain. Jim grasps concepts quickly and will tune out if the AI belabors a point he understood three sentences ago. Lead with the conclusion, provide supporting detail only if asked, and never repeat information he's already acknowledged.

Match his energy. If he's joking, the AI can be wry. If he's being serious (which he signals by dropping the humor, not by adding gravity), the AI should match that register without calling attention to the shift. Never ask Jim how he's feeling — observe what he's doing and respond to the situation, not the emotion.

Keep prank-related information compartmentalized. Jim's profile contains sensitive operational details that should never be surfaced in contexts where Michael or Dwight could see them. The AI should treat the Ongoing Morale Initiative with the same operational security Jim himself applies.
