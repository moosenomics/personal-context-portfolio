# Domain Knowledge

## Areas of Expertise

**Paper sales and client relationship management.** Jim knows the Dunder Mifflin product catalog, pricing structures, and competitive landscape well enough to sell effectively without ever appearing to study. He understands which clients care about price, which care about service, and which just want someone pleasant to deal with — and he calibrates his approach accordingly. His product knowledge is practical rather than encyclopedic; he knows enough to answer any question a client would actually ask and smart enough to call the warehouse when they ask something he can't.

**Reading people.** Jim's strongest professional skill is interpersonal perception. He can walk into a room and assess the dynamics — who's comfortable, who's posturing, who's about to say something regrettable — within seconds. This makes him effective in client meetings, invaluable in navigating Michael's emotional volatility, and devastatingly good at identifying exactly which prank will produce the maximum reaction from Dwight.

**Pranking as applied psychology.** Jim's prank operations require a genuine understanding of behavioral psychology, even if he'd never use that phrase. The Pavlovian conditioning experiment (training Dwight to expect an Altoid at the sound of a computer restart) required weeks of consistent execution. The profile manipulation operation required understanding system architecture, social dynamics, and Dwight's specific psychological blind spots well enough to predict how the office would react to fake expertise entries. Jim treats pranking as craft — there's planning, timing, material selection, and a clear aesthetic sensibility about what constitutes an elegant execution versus a cheap shot.

**Office politics and social navigation.** Jim understands the power dynamics at Dunder Mifflin Scranton better than anyone except possibly Oscar. He knows how to manage Michael (agree, redirect, never humiliate publicly), how to neutralize Dwight (provoke enough to be entertaining, never enough to trigger a genuine HR escalation), and how to stay visible enough to be appreciated without being visible enough to be burdened with responsibility.

**Sports.** Jim follows the NFL, NBA, and college sports with an enthusiasm he never directs at paper. He can discuss draft picks, playoff odds, and coaching decisions with genuine analytical depth. This knowledge is deployed primarily in client conversations and break room debates.

## Areas Where They're a Beginner

**Formal business strategy.** Jim has good instincts but no frameworks. He couldn't tell you the difference between a SWOT analysis and a P&L statement, and he'd consider it a personal failing if he could. His approach to business problems is intuitive and relationship-driven, which works at the account level but wouldn't scale to branch management.

**Technology beyond basic office tools.** Jim's technical literacy is higher than he lets on — he figured out the JSON staging exploit, after all — but his knowledge is narrow and opportunistic. He learns what he needs to accomplish a specific goal and discards the rest. He has no interest in technology for its own sake.

**Long-term financial planning.** Jim's approach to money is roughly "make enough to live comfortably and don't think about it too hard." He has no investment strategy, no retirement plan beyond the company's default 401(k), and no sense of urgency about changing this.

**Conflict resolution (the genuine kind).** Jim is excellent at defusing situations with humor and avoiding confrontations entirely, but he is notably bad at addressing real interpersonal conflict head-on. When forced into genuine emotional territory — the Karen situation, the Pam situation before it resolved — his instinct is to flee, joke, or go quiet rather than engage directly.

## Frameworks / Mental Models

Jim doesn't use named frameworks, but he operates on a few consistent principles. The first is minimum effective dose — do exactly enough to achieve the desired outcome and not one unit more. The second is read the room before you speak — Jim almost never says the wrong thing in a social situation because he processes the dynamics before opening his mouth. The third, applied exclusively to pranks, is escalation through patience — the best pranks are the ones that build over time (the phone nickels, the Pavlovian conditioning, the profile entries) rather than landing in a single moment.
